# Writing Craft Knowledge Base — Tier 2: Detailed Principles

A consolidated, de-duplicated reference distilled from seven books on writing well. Built for an LLM to use when **evaluating** prose (diagnosing problems), **creating** prose (drafting and revising), and **advising** writers.

The 304 individual rules extracted from the seven sources have been merged into **67 canonical principles**, organized under an 11-category taxonomy. Where the books agree, their advice is synthesized; where they genuinely disagree, the conflict is preserved under **Nuances & Disagreements** rather than resolved.

> **Tier 2 of 2 — Detailed Principles.** This file holds the full entry for every canonical principle. For the one-line scannable version, see the companion file `rules-index.md`. Each entry below is preceded by an anchor matching its ID (e.g., `#cc1`) so the index can link straight to it.

## Clarity & Concision

<a id="cc1"></a>
### CC1 — Omit needless words

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Padding, throat-clearing, redundant pairs; words a sentence survives without  
**Fix** (act): Cut every word that does no work; keep only those that aid parsing or rhythm.

**Principle**: Strip every sentence to its cleanest components. "A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines" (EoS). This does not mean making all sentences short — it means making every word tell.

**Why it matters**: Concision concentrates force; clutter — "the disease of American writing" (OWW) — dilutes it, hides meaning, and can be a tool of evasion. Each extra word imposes two costs on the reader: understanding it and fitting it into the sentence (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Replace padding with single words: *the question as to whether → whether*; *there is no doubt but that → no doubt*; *he is a man who → he*; *in a hasty manner → hastily.*
- Cut superfluous relatives and qualifiers: *who is, which was*; *a bit, sort of, in a sense.*
- Apply the bracket test (PRO6) and expect to cut a first draft by up to 50% (OWW).
- Combine a single complex idea spread across several sentences into one.

**Examples**:
- *Macbeth was very ambitious. This led him to wish to become king...* (55 words) → *Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth achieved his ambition... by murdering Duncan and becoming king of Scotland in his place.* (26 words) (EoS).
- *We have been experiencing considerable... expressions of dissatisfaction...* → *The students had been hassling us about different things.* (OWW).

**Common mistakes**: Keeping a useless phrase because it sounds beautiful; mistaking mere shortness for good concision.

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker (SoS) and Klinkenborg (SSS) qualify the maxim. Pinker: "omitting needless words does not mean cutting out every single word that is redundant" — keep little words (*that, which, the*) that prevent garden paths or fill out rhythm; difficulty depends on a sentence's geometry, not just its word count. Klinkenborg: don't try to fix a weak short sentence by making it longer; concision is a means to clarity and implication, not an end in itself.

**Sources**: EoS (Omit needless words; III.13); OWW (Simplicity; Clutter); Style (Concision, Lesson 8); SoS (Ch. 4); SSS (every word optional; break apart the crowded sentence).

<a id="cc2"></a>
### CC2 — Replace wordy phrases and empty abstraction-words with plain words

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Wordy phrases (the fact that, in the event that) and empty nouns (case, nature, level)  
**Fix** (act): Replace with one plain word; fold an announced topic into the subject.

**Principle**: Reduce set phrases and abstract filler nouns to the idea itself. A family of abstractions (*case, character, nature, factor, feature, system*) is "often simply redundant, used from a mere habit of wordiness" (EoS); "metaconcepts" like *level, perspective, approach, model, framework* are "packing material" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: These constructions add length without meaning and substitute vagueness for a concrete statement. "Could you recognize a 'level' or a 'perspective' if you met one on the street?" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- *In many cases, the rooms were poorly ventilated → Many of the rooms were poorly ventilated.*
- *Acts of a hostile character → Hostile acts.*
- *owing to the fact that → since*; *in the event that → if*; *prior to → before.*
- Work an announced topic into the subject: *In regard to a vigorous style, the most important feature is... → The most important feature of a vigorous style is...* (Style).

**Sources**: EoS (V: case, character, nature...); OWW (long words → short; Clutter); Style (phrase → word, Lesson 8); SoS (metaconcepts; obese phrases, Ch. 2 & 4).

<a id="cc3"></a>
### CC3 — Prefer affirmatives; state things positively

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Needless negatives; not + implicitly-negative words; stacked negation  
**Fix** (act): State positively (not honest->dishonest); set up a belief before denying it.

**Principle**: Make definite assertions. Use *not* for genuine denial or antithesis, not as a means of evasion. A negative statement is harder on the reader than an affirmative one, "because to hear or read a statement is to believe it, at least for a moment," and negating requires the extra step of tagging it false (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "The reader is dissatisfied with being told only what is not; he wishes to be told what is" (EoS). Stacked negatives — *not* plus implicitly negative words (*preclude, without, unless*), passives, and nominalizations — "baffle readers completely" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Convert to positive equivalents: *not honest → dishonest*; *did not remember → forgot*; *not different → the same*; *not allow → prevent.*
- *He was not very often on time → He usually came late* (EoS).
- To negate an unfamiliar idea, unveil it in two stages: "You might think... But no" (SoS).
- Nail down scope and focus: *All doors will not open → Not all doors will open*; never write "X not Y because Z" — keep *because* outside the negation (JFK: "not because it is easy but because it is hard").

**Nuances & disagreements**: Keep the negative when you specifically want to emphasize the negative (Style), or when a negative word other than *not* is itself strong ("The sun never sets...", EoS).

**Sources**: EoS (Put statements in positive form, III.12); Style (Lesson 8); SoS (Ch. 5, Negation).

<a id="cc4"></a>
### CC4 — Prune metadiscourse and signpost sparingly

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Verbiage about the text (this chapter discusses, it should be noted); over-signposting  
**Fix** (act): Cut metadiscourse; introduce topics with a question or the vision metaphor.

**Principle**: Metadiscourse is "verbiage about verbiage" — language about your own text (*this chapter discusses, it should be pointed out, the first thing to say is*). "Everything you write needs [some] metadiscourse, but too much buries your ideas" (Style). "Signpost sparingly, as we do in conversation" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: Heavy signposting reads "like a scrunched-up table of contents"; the reader works harder understanding the signposts than she saves. In one 34-word example only nine words address the actual subject (Style).

**How to apply**:
- *High divorce rates have been observed to occur in areas determined to have low population density → High divorce rates occur in areas with low population density* (Style).
- Introduce a topic with a question ("What makes a name rise and fall?") or the vision metaphor ("As we have seen...") rather than "this chapter discusses" (SoS).
- Keep metadiscourse that genuinely guides the reader (it clusters usefully in introductions and conclusions); cut the kind that merely attributes ideas to a vague source or announces the obvious.

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 8, Metadiscourse); SoS (Ch. 2).

<a id="cc5"></a>
### CC5 — Clear thinking precedes clear writing, and clarity is achievable

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=process · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Muddy logic, undefined aim, reader gets lost  
**Fix** (act): Clarify the thought first; ask What am I trying to say? Have I said it?

**Principle**: "Clear thinking becomes clear writing; one can't exist without the other" (OWW). The book-length conviction of Style: "it is good to write clearly, and anyone can do it." Obscurity is usually a failure of the writer, not a sign of deep ideas.

**Why it matters**: Turgid prose is "a language of exclusion that a democracy cannot tolerate" (Style). Readers who can't follow dense writing often blame themselves, then learn to write the same way. When a reader is lost, "it's usually because the writer hasn't been careful enough" (OWW). Per Wittgenstein, "whatever can be written can be written clearly" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Constantly ask: "What am I trying to say? Have I said it? Is it clear to someone meeting the subject for the first time?" (OWW).
- Distrust the two common defenses of opacity — that struggling to read makes us think harder, and that clarity "dumbs down" complex truths (Style).
- Recognize the **curse of knowledge** as the chief cause of unclear prose (see PRO2): you forget what it's like not to know what you know (SoS).

**Sources**: OWW (Simplicity); Style (Lessons 1 & 11); SoS (Ch. 3).

## Word Choice & Diction

<a id="wc1"></a>
### WC1 — Prefer the active voice, but choose the passive deliberately

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Agentless or perfunctory passives; there is/was openers; weak be-verbs  
**Fix** (act): Prefer active; use passive only to manage focus/flow or omit an unknown agent.

**Principle**: Default to the active voice — it is "more direct, bold, and vigorous" (EoS) and the difference in clarity and vigor is "the difference between life and death for a writer" (OWW). But the passive is a legitimate tool, not an error: use it on purpose.

**Why it matters**: A style built on passives "saps the reader's energy" (OWW) and lets writers hide who did what. Yet the passive rightly serves several jobs: managing old-before-new flow (it's "the biggest reason the passive is in the language," Style), keeping the spotlight on one character, omitting an unknown or irrelevant agent, and directing the reader's gaze "like a cinematographer choosing the best camera angle" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Replace perfunctory passives and *there is / could be heard* constructions with transitive active verbs: *Dead leaves covered the ground*, not *There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground* (EoS).
- Decide with three questions (Style): Must readers know who is responsible? Does active or passive flow better into the next sentence? Which gives a more consistent point of view?
- Use a strategic passive to keep a protagonist in subject position across sentences (SoS, the heron column).
- Combine all three verb types — active, passive, linking — each for its purpose (GoG, AS).

**Examples**: WWII "from the Allies' view": active ("the Allies had defeated Germany"); from Germany's view: passive ("Germany had been defeated") (Style). Tom French's chimp: passive as victim ("He was carried across an ocean..."), active as survivor ("He charmed Jane Goodall...") (GoG).

**Common mistakes**: Treating "avoid the passive" as an absolute rule (SoS calls the blanket ban "bad advice"); using the passive to evade responsibility ("mistakes were made").

**Sources**: EoS (III.11); OWW (Ch. 10, Verbs); Style (Lesson 3); SoS (Ch. 2 & 4); GoG (Ch. 33–34); AS (Ch. 3).

<a id="wc2"></a>
### WC2 — Put actions in strong, precise verbs; don't bury them in nominalizations

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Actions hidden in -tion/-ment nouns; abstract-noun subjects; empty verbs (make, conduct)  
**Fix** (act): Turn the action into a verb and the character into its subject.

**Principle**: "A sentence seems clear when its important actions are in verbs" (Style). Prose turns dense when actions are buried in abstract nouns — **nominalizations** (*-tion, -ment, -ence*), Pinker's "zombie nouns," Zinsser's "concept nouns." "All fine prose is based on the verbs carrying the sentences" (Fitzgerald, quoted in AS).

**Why it matters**: "No element of style more characterizes turgid academic and professional writing than lots of nominalizations" (Style). Zombie nouns "lumber across the scene without a conscious agent" and force the reader "to imagine an effect without a cause" (SoS). Concept-noun sentences "have no people in them and no working verbs — only 'is' or 'isn't'" (OWW).

**How to apply** (diagnose → analyze → rewrite, Style):
- Underline the first seven or eight words of each sentence; flag abstract-noun subjects.
- Identify the real characters and the actions hidden in their nouns.
- Turn nominalizations back into verbs and make the characters the subjects: *The committee's intention is to audit → The committee intends to audit*; *Prevention of neurogenesis diminished social avoidance → When we prevented neurogenesis, the mice no longer avoided other mice* (SoS).
- Recognize the five nominalization patterns (Style): nominalization as subject of an empty verb; following an empty verb; subject + a second following; after *there is*; or chained by prepositions.

**Nuances & disagreements**: Some nominalizations earn their keep — a short one referring back to the prior sentence aids cohesion (*These arguments...; The cancellation was unexpected*), and one can stand in for *the fact that* (Style, SoS). Tufte adds the positive case: a string of noun phrases can build cumulative force.

**Sources**: Style (Lessons 2–3); SoS (Ch. 2–3); OWW (Ch. 10, Concept Nouns / Creeping Nounism); AS (Ch. 2–3); GoG (Ch. 33).

<a id="wc3"></a>
### WC3 — Prefer concrete, sensory language and the telling detail over abstraction

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=word · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Abstractions, summaries, and labels where an image could show it  
**Fix** (act): Use concrete sensory wording and the telling detail; de-thingify variables.

**Principle**: Favor "fresh wording and concrete imagery over familiar verbiage and abstract summary" (SoS). A telling detail "obviates an explicit pronouncement" — show the reader rather than label. Replace "vague generality by definite statement" (EoS).

**Why it matters**: "Readers understand and remember material far better when it is expressed in concrete language that allows them to form visual images" (SoS). Concrete images "force us to keep updating the virtual reality display in our minds." Submerging people into abstractions ("forces," "processes") can even dehumanize (Orwell, via SoS).

**How to apply**:
- "De-thingify" variables: *There is a significant positive correlation between measures of food intake and body mass index → The more you eat, the fatter you get* (SoS).
- Wilkerson writes "when the land turned to dust," not "the Dust Bowl"; "the landless," not "the peasants" (SoS).
- Travel between the concrete and the abstract on the "ladder of abstraction"; flavor abstractions with specifics, and follow a general claim with particular evidence (GoG).
- Present the colorful fact and let readers marvel; don't over-explain or pre-judge it with *surprisingly, of course* (OWW; see PR5).

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 1 & 3); OWW (Trust Your Material); GoG (Ch. 47–48, ladder of abstraction, particularity); EoS (V headnote); SSS (know the names of things).

<a id="wc4"></a>
### WC4 — Most adjectives and adverbs are unnecessary — but a well-placed modifier carries the news

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Decorative adjectives and verb-duplicating adverbs (blared loudly)  
**Fix** (act): Delete habitual modifiers; keep only those carrying real information/news.

**Principle**: "Most adverbs are unnecessary" and "most adjectives are also unnecessary" (OWW): don't choose a precise verb and then add an adverb that repeats its meaning, and don't decorate nouns whose concept is already complete. Use intensifiers sparingly; "where emphasis is necessary, use words strong in themselves" (EoS).

**Why it matters**: "The adjective that exists solely as decoration is a self-indulgence for the writer and a burden for the reader" (OWW). Used sparsely, modifiers keep their power.

**How to apply**:
- Cut verb-duplicating adverbs: *blared loudly, grinned widely, totally flabbergasted* (OWW).
- Drop adjective-by-habit (*stately elms, lacy spiderwebs*); keep modifiers that add real information (*garish* daffodils as a judgment; *gray* sky and *black* clouds that explain a decision).
- Try deleting every adjective/adverb before a noun, then restore only the ones readers need (Style).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Tufte and Pinker push back on the blanket rule. After *be* and linking verbs especially, the adjective "carries the news and draws the stress" (AS); doubling or expanding a modifier can enlarge meaning (*He felt porous and pregnable*). Pinker calls "write with nouns and verbs, not adjectives and adverbs" a "badly stated" rule — a surprising, well-chosen modifier can electrify prose (see WC5).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 10, Adverbs/Adjectives); EoS (V, Very/So); Style (Lesson 8); AS (Ch. 4); SoS (Ch. 1).

<a id="wc5"></a>
### WC5 — Hunt for fresh, precise words; shun journalese, jargon, clichés, and fad words

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Cliches, journalese, jargon, fad words, unexplained terms  
**Fix** (act): Choose fresh precise words; explain or banish jargon; no single-use abbreviations.

**Principle**: "Journalese is the death of freshness in anybody's style" (OWW). Develop "a respect for words and a curiosity about their shades of meaning that is almost obsessive." Banish jargon and explain unavoidable technical terms (SoS); aim for a varied vocabulary and the occasional surprising word.

**Why it matters**: Clichés telegraph what's coming — "we are in the hands of a hack... We stop reading" (OWW). Jargon and abbreviations steal "minutes from the lives of readers" to save the writer seconds; explaining a term can "multiply readership a thousandfold at the cost of a handful of characters" (SoS). "The race in writing is not to the swift but to the original" (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Reject journalese coinages and padded verbs (*enthuse, beef up, upcoming, staffers*); reject pompous jargon that duplicates a plain word (*prioritize, input, disincentivize*).
- Spell out abbreviations on first use; use *for example, such as* liberally; coin no single-use abbreviations; *murine model → rats and mice* (SoS).
- Insert an unusual word judiciously — "dead-on in meaning, evocative in sound, and not so obscure that the reader will never see it again" (SoS); fancy words you half-understand make you look pompous (*pulchritudinous*).

**Sources**: OWW (Words; Usage; Sports; taste); SoS (Ch. 1 & 3); GoG (discourse communities; coinage); EoS (V, overworked expressions).

<a id="wc6"></a>
### WC6 — Use dictionaries and the thesaurus rightly; know word histories and near-synonym distinctions

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Show-offy thesaurus words; misused near-synonyms; words used by guess  
**Fix** (act): Use thesaurus to remind, dictionary to decide; check sense, history, pronunciation.

**Principle**: Use the thesaurus "to remind yourself of words you already know," not to dig up long fancy ones (GoG); then go to the dictionary to learn how near-synonyms differ (OWW). Read with a dictionary at hand (SoS). "The history of a word is part of its meaning" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: There are few true synonyms; precise distinctions are "signals to the reader that you are sensitive to the shadings of usage" (OWW). Inferring a word from context misleads (SSS). Word history "provides valuable context" for present choices (GoG, the OED/AHD).

**How to apply**:
- Master gradations among near-synonyms (*cajole, wheedle, blandish, coax*); weigh the emotional weight of *serene* vs. *tranquil* (OWW).
- Look up even familiar words "every time you have a doubt, and especially when you don't" — for meaning, etymology, and pronunciation, which bears on rhythm (SSS).
- Think of your vocabulary as your *autopsia* — words you've "seen for yourself" (SSS).

**Common mistakes**: Over-reliance on the thesaurus, producing show-offy prose (*masticate, delineate* for *chew, draw*); repeating a distinctive word on a page without reason ("word territory," GoG).

**Sources**: OWW (Words; Usage); SoS (Ch. 1); GoG (Ch. 1, 5); SSS (autopsia; tools the writer needs).

<a id="wc7"></a>
### WC7 — Weigh the sound and length of words

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Clotted Latinate diction; flat rhythm; unwanted repetition of sound  
**Fix** (act): Favor short Anglo-Saxon words at key moments; vary length; read for sound.

**Principle**: "Bear in mind, when you're choosing words and stringing them together, how they sound" (OWW). Readers hear prose more than writers realize, so "rhythm and alliteration are vital to every sentence." English carries a short Anglo-Saxon stock and a longer Latin/French stock; in general "the writer will find her way to short words and short sentences to say important and memorable things" (GoG).

**Why it matters**: "When short words are used in short sentences, the effect is like a power-loaded spring" (GoG); a short word after a litany of long ones lands hard (*bled out* vs. *exsanguinate*). Sound carries pleasure and emotional weight (E. B. White's test: "These are the times that try men's souls" survives rewording into "oatmeal"). Pronunciation "has a bearing on rhythm" (SSS).

**How to apply**:
- Put the most important thing in short words at points of highest emotion; mix word lengths to control pace and emphasis (GoG).
- Read everything aloud (PRO3); vary sentence openings and lengths so they don't "all sound as if they came out of the same mold" (OWW).
- Use sound devices — alliteration, assonance, onomatopoeia — for euphony, but "moderation is a virtue" (GoG).

**Sources**: OWW (Words; The Sound of Your Voice); GoG (Ch. 3, 10, 45); SSS (read aloud; tools).

## Sentence Craft

<a id="sc1"></a>
### SC1 — Master the short sentence; vary length and structure for rhythm

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Uniform sentence length/structure; long sentences that lose their thread  
**Fix** (act): Build from short sentences; vary length deliberately; one idea per sentence.

**Principle**: Learn to make short sentences and "force yourself to keep them short" (SSS); the short sentence restores "the directness of subject and verb" and makes ambiguity easier to detect. Then vary length and structure deliberately — "only a string of choppy sentences sounds choppy," and strong long sentences "are really just strong, short sentences joined in various ways" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: "An occasional short sentence can carry a tremendous punch. It stays in the reader's ear" (OWW). Variation "is the life of prose" and "a mark of the professional" (SSS, AS). Long sentences left unchecked "tend to collapse... so the end of the sentence commonly forgets its beginning" (SSS).

**How to apply**:
- Think only about length if most sentences run longer than thirty or shorter than fifteen words (Style); use short sentences for urgency or terse certainty, long ones for special effects.
- Turn every sentence into its own paragraph to see length and shape at a glance (SSS; see PRO6).
- Know the basic sentence forms — fragment, simple, complex, compound, compound-complex (GoG) — and the four short "kernel" patterns built on *be*, linking verbs, intransitives, and transitives (AS).
- Use the period as a "full stop": more periods, slower pace; the word before it is a hot spot (GoG).

**Sources**: SSS (short sentences; variation; rhythm); AS (Ch. 1, short sentences); Style (Lesson 10, length); OWW (Ch. 10, Punctuation); GoG (Ch. 12, 37).

<a id="sc2"></a>
### SC2 — Put the emphatic words at the end (the stress position)

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Sentence ends on a weak/light word; new info buried mid-sentence  
**Fix** (act): Move the emphatic/new/heavy element to the end (stress position).

**Principle**: "The proper place for the word... the writer desires to make most prominent is usually the end of the sentence" (EoS). Pinker's more precise version: **light-before-heavy** ("save the heaviest for last") and **given-before-new / topic-then-comment**. Williams calls the end the **stress position**; Tufte calls the principle "sentence perspective" — known first, new and climactic last.

**Why it matters**: End position gives prominence to the new, newsworthy element and "helps establish the voice readers hear" (Style). People "learn by integrating new information into their existing web of knowledge" and dislike a fact "hurled at them out of the blue" (SoS). End on slight words and the sentence "seems to end weakly" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Move the important element to the end: *This steel is principally used for making razors, because of its hardness → Because of its hardness, this steel is principally used in making razors* (EoS).
- Test by reading aloud and tapping hard on the last three or four words; if they don't deserve emphasis, revise (Style): trim the end, shift peripheral ideas left, shift new information right.
- Use Williams's six emphasis devices: *there*-shift, passive, *what*-shift, *it*-shift, *not only X but Y*, and pronoun/ellipsis. End elegantly on weighty words, *of* + a weighty word, or an echoing word (Style, Lesson 10).
- Reconcile rigid English order with these demands via passives, clefts (*It was Oedipus who...*), extraposition, and existentials (SoS).

**Sources**: EoS (III.18); Style (Lessons 5 & 10); SoS (Ch. 4); AS (Ch. 8, 13); OWW (Rewriting); GoG (Ch. 12).

<a id="sc3"></a>
### SC3 — Express coordinate ideas in parallel form

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Coordinate ideas in mismatched grammatical forms; broken correlatives  
**Fix** (act): Cast like ideas in like syntax; vary only the contrasting words.

**Principle**: "Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form" (EoS): expressions of similar content and function should be outwardly similar. "Parallelism is saying like things in like ways... by repetition of words and syntactic structures in planned symmetrical arrangements" (AS).

**Why it matters**: Parallel form makes the relationship of ideas visible; uneven form makes the writer seem "undecided or timid" (EoS). It lends "an unmistakable eloquence" (AS) and is, in Pinker's framing, "the Rule of One Variable applied to writing" — hold everything constant and vary only the contrasting words, so the reader can slot each phrase into the waiting structure.

**How to apply**:
- Keep parallel grammar across coordinate elements and after correlatives (*both...and, not only...but also, either...or*): *Either you must grant his request or incur his ill will → You must either grant his request or incur his ill will* (EoS).
- For similarity/contrast, vary only the words that carry the contrast (SoS).
- Balance for elegance — coordinate with *and, or, but, yet*, and make each succeeding element a bit longer than the last (Style).

**Common mistakes**: Faulty coordination — joining elements that aren't truly parallel; an unbalanced *both...and* (SoS).

**Sources**: EoS (III.15); AS (Ch. 12); Style (Lesson 10); SoS (Ch. 4–5).

<a id="sc4"></a>
### SC4 — Keep related words together — and pull apart unrelated phrases that attract

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Modifier far from its target; phrase attaches to the wrong nearby word  
**Fix** (act): Place related words together; pull apart unrelated phrases that attract.

**Principle**: "The position of the words in a sentence is the principal means of showing their relationship" (EoS): bring related words together, keep unrelated ones apart, and place modifiers next to what they modify. Subject and verb are "the king and queen of comprehensibility" and are "most powerful when they sit on adjacent thrones" (GoG).

**Why it matters**: Word position carries grammatical meaning in English; misplacement suggests wrong relations and breeds ambiguity.

**How to apply**:
- Don't needlessly separate subject and verb: *Wordsworth, in the fifth book of The Excursion, gives... → In the fifth book of The Excursion, Wordsworth gives...* (EoS).
- Put modifiers next to their target: *He only found two mistakes → He found only two mistakes* (EoS).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker refines Strunk's rule. Because readers attach a phrase to the nearest words, the better statement is "pull unrelated (but mutually attracted) phrases apart" — e.g., *a panel on drugs with four professors* misfires because *with four professors* attaches to *drugs*. So the goal isn't always proximity; it's preventing the wrong attachment. (See GP7 on misplaced modifiers.)

**Sources**: EoS (III.16); SoS (Ch. 4, attachment); GoG (Ch. 32).

---

<a id="sc5"></a>
### SC5 — Get to the subject and verb quickly and keep them close; avoid sprawl and center-embedding

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Long lead-ins, abstract subjects, interrupted subject-verb, center-embedding  
**Fix** (act): Get to a short concrete subject and verb fast; flatten/right-branch; split.

**Principle**: "You have to know how to write a sentence that is both long and clear" — length isn't the problem; sprawl is (Style). Get to the subject of the main clause quickly, then to the verb and object quickly. Spread the load with flat and **right-branching** structures; avoid bushy left-branching noun piles and especially center-embedded clauses (SoS).

**Why it matters**: Readers feel "shapeless length" when they wait too long for the verb or slog through tacked-on clauses (Style). A long right-branching sentence is easy to follow because "the reader never has to keep a phrase suspended in memory for long"; a multiply center-embedded one, "though grammatical, cannot be parsed by mortal humans" (SoS). The greater the subject–verb distance, the likelier an error or a confused reader (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- Carry the reader "from simplicity to complexity": short opener, short concrete subject, specific verb — then the sentence can run on (Style).
- Move a long introductory phrase to the end or make it its own sentence; don't interrupt the subject–verb or verb–object bond.
- "Disinter each embedded clause and place it side by side with the clause that contained it"; splitting one sentence into two or three is often the best fix (SoS). Break apart the overcrowded sentence (SSS).
- Know the branching options: right-branching makes meaning early; left-branching delays for drama; reserve middle-branching for a "happy interruption" (GoG).

**Exception**: The deliberate **periodic** (suspended) sentence piles up introductory clauses to heighten a stressed concluding main clause (Style; see SC6).

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 9, Shape); SoS (Ch. 4, sentence geometry); GoG (Ch. 32); SSS (break apart the crowded sentence).

<a id="sc6"></a>
### SC6 — Extend a sentence gracefully with free, resumptive, and summative modifiers

**Type**: Framework  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Sprawl from stacked which/that clauses  
**Fix** (act): Extend with resumptive, summative, or free modifiers around a clear base clause.

**Principle**: To lengthen a sentence without sprawl, "particularly avoid tacking one relative clause onto another onto another" (Style). Instead build cumulative, branching sentences around a clear base clause using **free modifiers** (set off by commas/dashes, modifying the whole clause or one word), attached left, right, or both (AS).

**Why it matters**: These devices let a writer "bring a clause to a sense of closure, then begin afresh" (Style), extending the line while keeping a clear nucleus (AS: Ellison's "Sensing a possible rival, I watched him warily, wondering who he was").

**How to apply** (Style's three modifiers):
- **Resumptive**: repeat a key noun/adjective/verb and continue with a *that*-clause.
- **Summative**: end a segment, add a noun summing it up, then a *that*-clause ("...a demographic event that will have serious social implications").
- **Free**: an *-ing*/participial or adjective phrase commenting on the subject, able to open or close the sentence.
- Reduce a potential clause to an appositive, absolute, infinitive, or participial phrase, mixing in an occasional full clause (AS). The appositive renames or adds information "in a natural and easy way."

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 9); AS (Ch. 7, 9, 10).

<a id="sc7"></a>
### SC7 — Treat syntax as a tool

**Type**: Principle  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=sentence · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Sentence design/architecture unclear (craft lens)  
**Fix** (act): Think in phrase-trees; reconcile information order with the word string.

**Principle**: Syntax "gives words the power to relate to each other in a sequence, to create rhythms and emphasis, to carry meaning" (AS). Grammar is "an app that uses a tree of phrases to translate a web of thoughts into a string of words" (SoS); word order does double duty as "a code for information, and a sequence of mental events."

**Why it matters**: Understanding the design behind a sentence — a linear string conveying a network of ideas — is "the key to understanding what you are trying to accomplish" (SoS). Studying professionals' use of "the generous resources of the English language" gives a writer "a systematic, comprehensive range of models" (AS).

**How to apply**:
- "Think in trees": apprehend how words group into phrases and zoom in on the heads, ignoring the clutter (SoS).
- Study exemplary sentences for their construction — the slot each phrase fills and the effect it creates — not just their words (AS).

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 4); AS (Foreword / Ch. 1).

---

<a id="sc8"></a>
### SC8 — Use the intentional fragment and varied sentence moods purposefully

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=sentence · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Monotone declaratives; accidental fragments  
**Fix** (act): Use intentional fragments and question/command/exclamation moods for effect.

**Principle**: The intentional fragment (verbless sentence) "can work for you and the reader, but only if you use it as a purposeful strategy" (GoG). Likewise the non-declarative moods — interrogative, imperative, exclamatory — are reshapings that "all make demands" and can set tone, accent a point, or structure paragraphs (AS).

**Why it matters**: Fragments can shock ("Cancer."), provide white space, build an inventory, or focus attention — but overdone they "get on the reader's nerves" (GoG). A direct or rhetorical question "imparts an urgency to the narrative" and keeps a story moving by focusing attention on an unresolved question (AS).

**How to apply**:
- Ensure a fragment's missing words are understood from context ("When hell freezes over!") (GoG).
- Deploy questions, commands, and exclamations sparingly, for transition, summary, or dramatic pitch (AS). (For the question as a narrative engine, see NS3.)

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 38); AS (Ch. 11).

---

<a id="sc9"></a>
### SC9 — Work at the level of the sentence; know what each says, doesn't say, and implies

**Type**: Principle  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=sentence · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Sentences that over-explain or say less/more than intended  
**Fix** (act): Make each sentence say exactly its thing; leave room for implication.

**Principle**: "Know what each sentence says, what it doesn't say, and what it implies. Of these, the hardest is knowing what each sentence actually says" (SSS). "The only link between you and the reader is the sentence you're making." Aim to **write by implication** — "the ability to suggest more than the words seem to allow, the ability to speak to the reader in silence."

**Why it matters**: Readers believe your sentences, not your intentions; "every sentence has its own motives... quite apart from yours." "The longer the sentence, the less it's able to imply," so room for implication comes from cutting extraneous words. "Shape, form, structure, genre, the whole — these have a way of clarifying themselves when sentences become clear."

**How to apply**:
- Make each sentence "take the stage, say the very thing it comes into existence to say, then leave the stage" — without talking about what it's saying.
- Leave space between sentences "for the things that words can't really say"; pay attention to rhythm, first and last.
- Recognize that thought and sentence are a collaboration: "the sentence becomes the thought by bringing it fully into being" — so making sentences is a way of discovering what you didn't know you could say.

**Sources**: SSS (the sentence as unit; write by implication; thought and sentence).

## Structure & Organization

<a id="so1"></a>
### SO1 — Make the paragraph a unit of one topic; keep paragraphs short

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=paragraph · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Topic-mixed or wall-of-text paragraphs  
**Fix** (act): One topic per paragraph; keep them short; break where a new step begins.

**Principle**: "Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic" (EoS); as a rule, begin each with a topic sentence and end in conformity with it. Keep paragraphs short — "writing is visual; it catches the eye before it... catches the brain" (OWW) — but "don't go berserk" with a string of tiny ones.

**Why it matters**: The start of each paragraph signals the reader that a new step has been reached (EoS). Short paragraphs "put air around what you write and make it look inviting" (OWW); a long block discourages reading, while midget paragraphs chop up a natural train of thought.

**How to apply**:
- Relate the body of a paragraph to its topic sentence by restating, defining, illustrating, or showing consequences (EoS).
- In dialogue, start a new paragraph with each change of speaker (EoS).
- Give each paragraph "its own integrity of content and structure" — a road map of your organization (OWW).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker denies any fixed "paragraph" unit: use the break as "a visual bookmark" that lets readers rest — "carve the notch above a sentence that does not elaborate or follow from the one before," and don't trowel out "massive slabs" (SoS).

**Sources**: EoS (III.9–10); OWW (Ch. 10, Paragraphs); SoS (Ch. 5).

---

<a id="so2"></a>
### SO2 — State the point early; don't bury the lede

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Buried lede; the point arrives late  
**Fix** (act): State the point at the end of the opening segment, at every level.

**Principle**: "Begin each document, its sections, and subsections with a short, easily grasped segment that states the point and introduces the themes" (Style). A writer "has to have both something to talk about (the topic) and something to say (the point)," stated "not too far from the beginning" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "A point clearly stated up front gives us a context to understand the complexity that follows"; when delayed, "we have to reconstruct it" (Style). In the Bransford & Johnson experiment, telling readers the topic *before* they read doubled recall; telling them after didn't help (SoS). "Point sentences constitute the outline of your document... If readers miss them, they may judge your writing incoherent" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Put the point sentence at the end of the short opening segment — the last thing read before the complex part begins (Style); the principle scales from clause to paragraph to whole document.
- "Don't bury the lede" — resist false suspense, professional narcissism, or the habit of discovering your point only while drafting (the "shoot the arrow, then paint the target" cartoon) (SoS).

**Nuances & disagreements**: This is in direct tension with Klinkenborg's "Anxiety of Sequence" (see SO11), which rejects the premise that meaning must arrive at a fixed point. The two can be reconciled: state your point early *for the reader* even if you discovered it last.

**Sources**: Style (Lessons 7 & 9); SoS (Ch. 5); OWW (The Lead); EoS (III.10).

---

<a id="so3"></a>
### SO3 — Cohesion: link sentences old-before-new

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=paragraph · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Sentences don't link; new info up front, old info at the end  
**Fix** (act): End each sentence with what sets up the next (old-before-new).

**Principle**: "Sentences are cohesive when the last few words of one set up information that appears in the first few words of the next" (Style). Begin sentences with familiar information; end with what the reader can't predict — given before new (SoS, AS).

**Why it matters**: This arrangement "is what gives us our experience of flow" (Style) and is a major contributor to coherence (SoS). Of the core clarity principles — characters as subjects, actions as verbs, old before new — "if you have to choose among them, favor the third," because "a passage's overall cohesion trumps the clarity of individual sentences" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Use a passive or a rearranging construction when it puts old information first and pushes new material to the end (Style; SoS, the Oedipus passives).
- Combine repetition with variety in syntax, sentence length, and openers/endings (AS).
- It's hard to spot in your own writing because, after working on your ideas, "they all seem familiar — to you" (Style).

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 4); SoS (Ch. 4); AS (Ch. 13, Cohesion).

---

<a id="so4"></a>
### SO4 — Coherence: use consistent subjects/topics and run theme-strings through a passage

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=document · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Unrelated subjects across sentences; theme words drift  
**Fix** (act): Use consistent subjects/topics; run a transparent theme-string through the passage.

**Principle**: Cohesion (sentence-to-sentence fit) is not coherence (the sense that sentences "add up"). For coherence, "in most of your sentences, use subjects to name their topics," keep those topics "a narrow set of related ideas," and refer to each running theme "in a consistent way" (Style, SoS).

**Why it matters**: A passage can be locally cohesive yet globally incoherent if its subjects are unrelated (the "mashed-potatoes" paragraph, Style). "It's always easier for a reader to follow a narrative if he can keep his eyes on a protagonist who is moving the plot forward" (SoS, the heron column). A coherent text is "a designed object... crisscrossed by arcs that track topics, points, actors, and themes" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Diagnose by underlining the first seven or eight words of every sentence; ask whether they form a small set of related ideas naming the main characters (Style).
- Avoid "throat-clearing" — metadiscourse, attitude words, and time/place phrases stacked before the subject (Style).
- Localize each theme in a string of consecutive paragraphs with a transparently related cluster of words (SoS, Mueller's "criminal" vs. "disciplined" warfare).
- Put key themes in the stress position of a passage's opening sentence (Style).

**Sources**: Style (Lessons 4–5, 7); SoS (Ch. 5, topic strings & thematic consistency).

---

<a id="so5"></a>
### SO5 — Open strong

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=draft · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Cliche/banal opening that doesn't provoke curiosity  
**Fix** (act): Open with a contentful, curiosity-raising first sentence already moving at speed.

**Principle**: "The most important sentence in any article is the first one" (OWW): the lead must capture the reader "with freshness, or novelty, or paradox, or humor, or surprise." Good writing "starts strong — not with a cliché ('Since the dawn of time'), not with a banality, but with a contentful observation that provokes curiosity" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "If [the first sentence] doesn't induce the reader to proceed to the second sentence, your article is dead" (OWW). Readers want to know "very soon what's in it for them."

**How to apply**:
- After hooking, make the lead "do some real work": why the piece exists and why to read on — without dwelling (OWW).
- Make each sentence pull the reader to the next; give the last sentence of each paragraph an "extra twist" (the "springboard," OWW).
- Avoid stale lead templates (the visitor from Mars, "one day not long ago") (OWW).
- Klinkenborg's variant: "begin the piece, not introduce it" — audition many first sentences and pick one "already moving at speed"; "the reader doesn't need grabbing... She needs to feel your interest in the sentence" (SSS).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 9); SoS (Ch. 1); SSS (how to begin); Style (Lesson 6, motivation).

---

<a id="so6"></a>
### SO6 — Finish strong, and stop when you're done

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Summary cranking; the piece runs past its natural end  
**Fix** (act): Stop when done; end on a strong, slightly surprising, exactly-right note.

**Principle**: "When you're ready to stop, stop" (OWW). Give the last sentence almost as much thought as the first; "the perfect ending should take your readers slightly by surprise and yet seem exactly right." "Good writing finishes strong" and lingers (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "An article that doesn't stop where it should stop becomes a drag and therefore a failure"; summary endings ("In sum, it can be noted...") make the reader hear "the laborious sound of cranking" and quit (OWW). A strong finish lingers — Wilkerson ends a chapter "with a paragraph composed of exactly two syllables" ("They left."), mirroring the finality (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Once the facts are in and the point made, "look for the nearest exit" (OWW); escape the school I/II/III summary.
- Effective endings bring the story full circle, end on a quotation with a sense of finality, or end on surprise (OWW).
- A conclusion can restate the point, answer "So what?" again, and pose a further question ("Now what?") (Style).
- Klinkenborg: often "you've already written the ending you need" — the reader saw it coming "from miles away" (SSS).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 9; A Writer's Decisions); SoS (Ch. 1); Style (Lesson 6); SSS (endings).

---

<a id="so7"></a>
### SO7 — Motivate the reader by posing a problem

**Type**: Framework  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=draft · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Intro states a topic but no problem or stakes (so what?)  
**Fix** (act): Pose a problem: shared context -> condition + cost -> solution.

**Principle**: "See yourself as posing a problem that your readers want to see solved," not just "writing about a topic" (Style). The standard introduction has three parts: **Shared Context → Problem → Solution/Main Point**, where a problem is a **condition** plus an **intolerable cost** the reader wants to avoid.

**Why it matters**: A topic-only introduction invites a shrug ("Who cares that college students drink a lot?"). "You get just one shot" at overcoming the reader's "So what?" — in the introduction (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Find the cost by imagining a reader asking "So what?" after your statement of the condition, and answering it.
- Distinguish **practical problems** (a condition in the world; solution = an action) from **conceptual problems** (something we don't understand; solution = a finding/claim). Connect a small answerable question to a larger one readers care about (Style).
- A common experienced-writer opening: "open with a seeming truth, then qualify or even reject it" (the shared context, then *but...*). An optional prelude (quotation, startling fact, anecdote) can introduce themes (Style). This dovetails with SoS's "reveal the topic and the point early" (SO2).

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 6); SoS (Ch. 5).

<a id="so8"></a>
### SO8 — Maintain unity of person, tense, and tone

**Type**: Framework  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Shifts in person, tense, or tone  
**Fix** (act): Fix unities up front; hold one pronoun, tense, and tone (or shift on purpose).

**Principle**: "Unity is the anchor of good writing" (OWW): choose and hold unities of pronoun (1st/2nd/3rd person), tense, and mood/tone. "Any tone is acceptable. But don't mix two or three." In summaries, "keep to one tense" (EoS).

**Why it matters**: Unity "keeps the reader from straggling off in all directions" and "satisfies the readers' subconscious need for order" (OWW). Shifting tense "gives the appearance of uncertainty and irresolution" (EoS); unintended shifts cause disorientation (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- Before starting, decide: in what capacity, what pronoun and tense, what style, what attitude, how much to cover, what one point to make (OWW).
- If the material leads somewhere truer, follow it — then rewrite the opening so mood and style are consistent start to finish (OWW).
- Prefer the historical past tense as your "workhorse"; shift to present only "with a purpose and a plan" (GoG).

**Sources**: OWW (Unity); EoS (III.17); GoG (Ch. 35).

<a id="so9"></a>
### SO9 — Order material logically and signal the order — but connect just once

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Missing or wrong connectives; order not visible; doubled connectors  
**Fix** (act): Signal logical/temporal/coordinate order, once each. (Genre-sensitive: see policy.)

**Principle**: Readers "must see how the parts of your document are ordered" (Style). Signal the order — chronological, coordinate, or logical — with appropriate connectives (*first, also, on the other hand, it follows that*). Because the curse of knowledge makes writers under-explain, "there is a greater danger of prose being confusing because it has too few connectives... When in doubt, connect" — but "if you do indicate a connection, do it just once" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "A coherent text is one in which the reader always knows which coherence relation holds between one sentence and the next" (SoS). Mood-changer words placed early (*but, yet, however, meanwhile*) make a sentence far easier to process (OWW). Redundant connectives (*the reason is because*) send readers hunting for a nonexistent second point (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Use Hume's three relation-families — resemblance, contiguity (sequence), cause/effect — and signal them with *because, so, but*; use parallel syntax for similarity/contrast (SoS).
- Alert the reader to a change of direction "as soon as possible"; place *however* early, not trailing "like a wet dishrag" (OWW).
- For difficult/linear material, lead the reader step by step so "every step should seem inevitable" (OWW, science writing).

**Nuances & disagreements**: This is the file's sharpest cross-source conflict — see SO11 and PR6. Klinkenborg argues the opposite: transitions are mostly "the nervous stitching together of sentences," logical indicators (*in fact, therefore, moreover, however*) "insist upon logic whether it exists or not" and "betray the writer's anxiety," and a truly well-ordered piece "needs no logical indicators." All four writers agree, however, that *but* is preferable to *however*, and that you may begin a sentence with *but*.

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 7); SoS (Ch. 5, connectives); OWW (Mood Changers; Science); contrast SSS (cut the logical indicators; transitions).

<a id="so10"></a>
### SO10 — Narrow the scope; make one main point

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=draft · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Scope too broad; multiple competing points  
**Fix** (act): Narrow to one corner of the subject; leave the reader one main thought.

**Principle**: "Every writing project must be reduced before you start to write" (OWW): decide what corner of the subject to bite off, cover it, and stop. "Every successful piece of nonfiction should leave the reader with one provocative thought" — just one.

**Why it matters**: The "definitiveness complex" sends writers chasing every fact and never settling; "there is no last word." An unwieldy task drains the enthusiasm that keeps readers reading (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Make reductive decisions about time, place, and characters ("one man pursuing one whale").
- For memoir, "think narrow" — a window into a life, not a summary (OWW).
- Decide the single point you want to leave in the reader's mind; it shapes route, destination, tone, and attitude.

**Sources**: OWW (Unity; The Memoir).

<a id="so11"></a>
### SO11 — Plan the structure, or discover it as you write

**Type**: Guideline (genuine disagreement)  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Structure forced, or absent (process)  
**Fix** (act): Discover structure while drafting; impose a clear point-first order in revision.

**Principle**: The books split on how structure should arise.

**Position A — plan and signpost (Style, OWW, SoS).** Resolve big decisions before writing — shape, structure, focus, intention (OWW); organize content into an anticipatable hierarchy and "lay an intuitive trail through the territory," using outlines, "a tree on its side" (SoS). Cookie-cutter structure "earns the gratitude of readers who have too little time" (Style).

**Position B — discover it sentence by sentence (SSS).** "Writing comes from writing." An outline "prevents discovery within the act of writing"; "you'll never know what you think until you escape your outline." Reject "the Anxiety of Sequence" — the school premise that you must begin in the right place knowing where you'll conclude. "You can get anywhere from anywhere... There is no single necessary order"; "form is discovery too."

**How to reconcile**: The disagreement is mostly about *drafting*, less about the *reader's* final experience. Even Klinkenborg wants the finished prose clear; even Williams concedes a personal literary essay may set the patterns aside. A workable synthesis: discover your structure freely while drafting (B), then revise so the reader meets a clear, point-first order (A, SO2). Note Klinkenborg's own concession that a partial outline is fine "as long as you're prepared to abandon it."

**Related**: SO2 (state the point early), SO9 (signal order), PR6 (attest vs. prove), PRO1 (revise).

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 7); OWW (The Tyranny of the Final Product; A Writer's Decisions); SoS (Ch. 5); SSS (abandon the outline; anxiety of sequence; you can get anywhere from anywhere).

## Voice & Tone

<a id="vt1"></a>
### VT1 — Be yourself; develop one recognizable voice

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Borrowed or ornamented voice; generic tone  
**Fix** (act): Be yourself; pursue clarity and let one recognizable voice emerge.

**Principle**: "Be yourself" (OWW): style is organic to the writer, "as much a part of him as his hair" — you cannot buy it and drape it on. Develop "one voice that readers will recognize" and "don't alter your voice to fit your subject." Klinkenborg's complement: "Pursue clarity instead. In the pursuit of clarity, style reveals itself."

**Why it matters**: "Ultimately the product that any writer has to sell is... who he or she is" (OWW); readers "want the person who is talking to them to sound genuine." Chasing "style" directly produces self-conscious "bow ties of self"; it has "more to do with what the writer makes of himself than how the reader experiences his prose" (SSS).

**How to apply**:
- First master the carpentry — "strip your writing down before you can build it back up" (OWW); to be yourself you must relax and have confidence.
- Avoid the three voice-cheapeners: breeziness, condescension, and clichés (OWW).
- Don't think about "style" while drafting; "you have more important things to think about — like revision" (SSS). Real style shows in "the character of your thinking... your sense of humor or irony."

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker reframes "voice" as scenario: "the key to good style... is to have a clear conception of the make-believe world in which you're pretending to communicate" (writing is "an act of pretense," SoS). Different scenarios call for different styles — see VT4.

**Sources**: OWW (Style; The Sound of Your Voice; Develop one voice); SSS (pursue clarity; style reveals itself); SoS (Ch. 2).

---

<a id="vt2"></a>
### VT2 — Write as if in conversation; use *I* and *you*

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Impersonal 'one' / institutional passive; no reader address  
**Fix** (act): Write as conversation; use I/you; show the reader something concrete.

**Principle**: "Writers are obviously at their most natural when they write in the first person" (OWW); use *I, we, you*, or at least retain "a sense of I-ness." Pinker's **classic style** is the guiding model: "prose is a window onto the world" — the writer shows the reader something real and engages her in conversation.

**Why it matters**: "Writing is an intimate transaction between two people, conducted on paper" (OWW). *I/me/you* "simulate a conversation" and are "gifts to the memory-challenged reader" (SoS). Classic style "makes the reader feel like a genius. Bad writing makes the reader feel like a dunce," and is "the strongest cure" for academese and bureaucratese (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Don't hide behind "one finds oneself" or institutional passives ("initiatives were undertaken") (OWW).
- Treat abstract ideas "as if they were objects and forces recognizable to anyone standing in a position to see them" (SoS).
- In scholarly prose, *I/we* with metadiscourse verbs (*show, argue, claim*) is fine; the "objective" passive "doesn't make a researcher's thinking more objective" (Style).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 4, Style); SoS (Ch. 2, classic style); Style (Lesson 3).

---

<a id="vt3"></a>
### VT3 — Be confident; make definite assertions; hedge by choice, not by reflex

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Compulsive hedges (sort of, I would argue) or bulldozer intensifiers  
**Fix** (act): Assert definitely; hedge only by deliberate choice; qualify, don't fudge.

**Principle**: "Sell yourself, and your subject will exert its own appeal... writing is an act of ego" (OWW). Avoid compulsive hedging — "wads of fluff" (*almost, apparently, fairly, somewhat, I would argue*). "It's not that good writers never hedge their claims. It's that their hedging is a choice, not a tic" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "Every little qualifier whittles away some fraction of the reader's trust" (OWW). Hedges and intensifiers signal "how well you balance caution and confidence" and "influence how readers judge your character" (Style): too much hedging "sounds mealy-mouthed," over-intensifying "come[s] on like a bulldozer." "Leaders who bob and weave like aging boxers don't inspire confidence — or deserve it. The same... is true of writers" (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Prune little qualifiers (*a bit, sort of, rather, very, pretty much*); "Be confused. Be tired... Don't be kind of bold. Be bold" (OWW).
- When precision genuinely matters, *qualify* (spell out the exceptions) rather than vaguely hedge; state magnitude and certainty explicitly (SoS). Note that *very* often weakens — "substitute *damn* every time you're inclined to write *very*."
- State a claim moderately and "readers are more likely to consider it thoughtfully"; even Watson and Crick hedged ("We wish to suggest a structure...") (Style).

**Related**: Strunk's "put statements in positive form" (CC3) and "use intensifiers sparingly" (WC4); Klinkenborg's "muster your own authority" (it "arises from the way you write, not from the subject," and "the first person who needs to be persuaded of your authority is you").

**Sources**: OWW (Style; Little Qualifiers); EoS (III.12; V); SoS (Ch. 2); Style (Lesson 8); SSS (authority).

<a id="vt4"></a>
### VT4 — Match register and formality to the occasion; use contractions when they fit

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Register mismatched to the occasion; stilted lack of contractions  
**Fix** (act): Match formality to context; use contractions where they fit.

**Principle**: "Every writer commands a range of styles" (SoS). Using an informal style where formal is wanted seems "flippant"; using formal where informal is wanted seems "stuffy, pompous, affected." "Both kinds of mismatch are errors" — but many supposed grammar errors are merely matters of register. "Your style will be warmer and truer... if you use contractions" (OWW).

**Why it matters**: Many "rules" (starting with *and/but/because*; flat adverbs like *drive safe*; *ain't* in a homespun saying) are register choices, not grammatical mistakes (SoS). Contractions sound natural read aloud; their absence sounds stilted (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Choose register by audience and occasion, not by fear; don't confuse informality with incorrectness (SoS).
- Use contractions that "fit comfortably," but avoid ambiguous *I'd/he'd* and invented forms like *could've* (OWW).
- For short digital forms (email, posts, texts), an effective informal style still demands "certain formal strategies" — Q&A, short words, purposeful fragments, sharp focus (GoG); master slang and dialect as register tools (NS5).

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 6, formality vs. grammar); OWW (Contractions; Sound of Your Voice); GoG (Ch. 50, 41–42).

---

<a id="vt5"></a>
### VT5 — Use inclusive, non-sexist language smoothly

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Male-default or patronizing terms; clunky he/she fixes  
**Fix** (act): Use inclusive terms or the singular they smoothly.

**Principle**: Eliminate sexist usages — patronizing words and the male-default — but without constructions that "clog the language" (OWW). "Gender equality trumps the arithmetic logic of formal grammar," and the increasingly accepted singular *they* often reads best (GoG).

**Why it matters**: Sexist words carry "an overtone of judgment" and make women readers "visualize a man doing the writing." But overcorrection — "a style that converts every 'he' into a 'they' will quickly turn to mush" (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Recast to the plural, reword, offer *he or she* sparingly, or use the singular *they* (GoG).
- Find generic terms with "practical elegance": *flight attendant, firefighter, police officer, company representative* (OWW, GoG).
- Dissolve the male pronoun with *we/you/the* or a general noun: *Doctors often neglect their wives and children → ...their families* (OWW).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 10, Sexism); GoG (Ch. 25).

---

<a id="vt6"></a>
### VT6 — Don't overstate; protect credibility; favor restraint

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Overstatement; scare quotes; exclamation on an already-strong fact  
**Fix** (act): Understate; drop scare quotes; let the fact land on its own.

**Principle**: "Don't overstate" (OWW): don't inflate an incident, and "let the humor sneak up so we hardly hear it coming." Drop scare quotes — "classic style is confident about its own voice" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "Credibility is just as fragile for a writer as for a President"; one bogus statement makes "everything you write thereafter suspect" (OWW). Scare quotes signal "please don't think I'm a flibbertigibbet" and breed ambiguity; "if you're not comfortable using an expression without apologetic quotation marks, you probably shouldn't be using it at all" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Resist verbal high jinks ("the living room looked as if an atomic bomb had gone off") (OWW).
- Let a startling fact land on its own, without an exclamation point that spoils the reader's discovery (OWW; see PR5).
- Reserve quotation marks for their legitimate jobs — quoting, mentioning a word as a word, disavowing others' usage (SoS).

**Sources**: OWW (Overstatement; Credibility); SoS (Ch. 2, scare quotes).

<a id="vt7"></a>
### VT7 — Enact sincerity; be the narrator as a deliberate dramatic role

**Type**: Principle  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Flat or insincere persona; sincerity merely asserted  
**Fix** (act): Enact the stance in the sentences; inhabit a deliberate narrator role.

**Principle**: "In writing, it's impossible to express sincerity sincerely. If you want the reader to feel your sincerity, your sentences have to enact sincerity — verbally, syntactically, even rhythmically" (SSS). "You be the narrator... Being the narrator is not the same as being yourself. It's a role, and a dramatic one."

**Why it matters**: "Writing is always a gesture requiring your dramatic presence," made of rhetorical choices about who you are to subject and reader. "You're never merely, sincerely yourself. The question then becomes, who are you? That's a question every piece needs to answer."

**How to apply**:
- "Absorb and inhabit" the narrator's role; "you're always building a habitation in your prose, a place from which you speak to the reader."
- Recognize that the clarity that reads as sincerity "isn't natural. It's artificial, the result of hard work."

**Related**: Complements VT1 (be yourself) by reframing "yourself" as a constructed persona, and VT2/SoS's "writing is an act of pretense."

**Sources**: SSS (sincerity; be the narrator).

## Grammar, Punctuation & Mechanics

<a id="gp1"></a>
### GP1 — Treat rules as tools: master them, then break them with purpose; tell real rules from myths

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=process · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): A rule applied dogmatically, or a myth-rule enforced (judgment)  
**Fix** (act): Keep real conventions; ignore myths (look it up); break tools with purpose.

**Principle**: "Embrace grammar... not as a set of rules but as a box of tools" (GoG); "once mastered, these 'rules' become 'tools' and their violation becomes strategic" — but "first you must learn them." Usage rules are "tacit conventions," not logical truths or edicts ("when it comes to correct English, there's no one in charge") (SoS). The point of grammar "isn't correctness... It's keeping the rules from obtruding upon the reader" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: Real conventions "lubricate comprehension, reduce misunderstanding, and signal that a writer has exercised care" (SoS); but phony rules cause "ham-fisted copyediting and smarty-pants one-upmanship." "You will often be judged, fairly or unfairly, on your use of language" (GoG).

**How to apply** — distinguish a real rule from a myth (SoS, "bubbe meises"): the simplest test is **look it up** in a modern usage guide. Keep a rule if it extends an intuitive grammatical pattern, if careful writers agree once a breach is pointed out, and if it marks a useful distinction. **Blow it off** if it rests on a crackpot theory (English must emulate Latin), is refuted by the facts of English, began as a maven's pet peeve, was flouted by great writers, makes the sentence clumsier, or merely confuses grammar with formality.
- Beware the **etymological fallacy** (only a word's original sense is "correct") and the **logical fallacy** (proper usage must be more logical) (SoS).
- Politely ignore others' "crotchets" while staying sensitive to a teacher's or editor's preferences (GoG).

**Nuances & disagreements**: A spectrum of strictness. Strunk is the most rule-bound — yet even he says the best writers break rules only when they gain "some compensating merit," so "unless you are certain of doing as well, follow the rules" (EoS). Zinsser splits the difference: "liberal in accepting new words... but conservative in grammar," keeping classic distinctions while welcoming useful coinages (OWW). Pinker and Clark are the most permissive about myth-rules (split infinitives, sentence-final prepositions, *and*/*but* openers). All converge on: learn the conventions, then deviate deliberately.

**Sources**: GoG (Intro; Part Three; Ch. 36); SoS (Ch. 6); SSS (purpose of correctness); OWW (Usage); EoS (I; "follow the rules unless you can do better").

<a id="gp2"></a>
### GP2 — Form the possessive singular with *'s*; let the ear guide awkward sibilants

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Possessive errors; dropped 's after a sibilant; its vs it's  
**Fix** (act): Add 's (Charles's); use 'the X of Y' for awkward sibilants; its != it's.

**Principle**: "Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding *'s*, whatever the final consonant" — *Charles's, Burns's, the witch's* (EoS). Clark endorses this "because it echoes the way we would speak the word aloud."

**Why it matters**: A single consistent rule removes guesswork and matches major style authorities (EoS); a dropped *s* (AP's *Agnes'*) creates "discord between my eye and ear" (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- Exceptions: ancient names in *-es/-is*, *Jesus'*, and *for conscience' sake*.
- For awkward sibilants, use the prepositional escape hatch: *the heel of Achilles, the teachings of Socrates* (EoS, GoG).
- Pronominal possessives take no apostrophe: *hers, its, theirs, yours.*

**Common mistakes**: *it's* (contraction) for *its* (possessive) — one of the "unforgivable" errors (see GP5).

**Sources**: EoS (II.1); GoG (Ch. 16).

---

<a id="gp3"></a>
### GP3 — Use the serial (Oxford) comma

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Missing serial comma in a series  
**Fix** (act): Comma before the final and/or (per house style).

**Principle**: "In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last": *red, white, and blue* (EoS). "The longer the elements in a series, the more likely you will need the serial comma" (GoG).

**Why it matters**: It prevents ambiguity and keeps "each element of meaning in its place"; the final comma also "alerts me that I'm coming to the end of the list" (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- Use it for words, phrases, or clauses.
- Know your house style first (AP drops it; literary publishers keep it) before deviating (GoG). In firm names, omit the last comma (*Brown, Shipley and Company*) (EoS).

**Sources**: EoS (II.2); GoG (Ch. 13).

<a id="gp4"></a>
### GP4 — Master restrictive vs. nonrestrictive (commas; *that* vs. *which*)

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Restrictive clause set off by commas, or vice versa; that/which misuse  
**Fix** (act): Commas only for nonessential elements; that=restrictive, which=nonrestrictive.

**Principle**: Set off nonrestrictive (nonessential) elements with commas; use no commas for restrictive (essential) ones — "if the phrase is not essential, then a comma or commas are essential" (GoG). The real *that/which* decision is restrictive (no commas) vs. nonrestrictive (commas), not a "literary" preference (OWW, SoS).

**Why it matters**: "The smallest of decisions — comma versus no comma — can mean a world of difference" (GoG): *Penn's daughter Margaret* (more than one daughter) vs. *Penn's daughter, Margaret* (one). Commas distinguish *Susan visited her friend Teresa* from *...her friend, Teresa* (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Test: can the sentence make sense without the phrase? If yes, set it off with commas (GoG).
- Never omit one of a pair of parenthetic commas — a single stray comma is "indefensible" (EoS).
- "Always use *that* unless it makes your meaning ambiguous"; if the sentence needs a comma, it probably needs *which* (OWW).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker stresses the ban on restrictive *which* is "utterly incorrect" (a 1926 Fowler invention) — *that* is a fine default (copy editors prefer it), but *which* is also correct and sometimes mandatory. The substance everyone agrees on is the comma/meaning distinction, not the word choice.

**Sources**: EoS (II.3); OWW (That and Which); GoG (Ch. 27); SoS (Ch. 6); Style (Lesson 4).

---

<a id="gp5"></a>
### GP5 — Avoid comma splices, run-ons, and the few "unforgivable" punctuation errors

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Comma splices, run-ons, it's/its, comma between subject and predicate  
**Fix** (act): Join clauses with semicolon/period/conjunction; avoid the unforgivable errors.

**Principle**: Two independent clauses not joined by a conjunction take a semicolon or period, not a comma (EoS). A handful of errors "have become tantamount to the confession 'I am illiterate'... no writer should be caught making them": the run-on sentence, the comma splice, the grocer's apostrophe, the comma between subject and predicate, and the possessive *it's* (SoS).

**Why it matters**: These few errors disproportionately damage a writer's credibility (SoS); the semicolon signals coordinate independent statements, while a comma blurs the sentence boundary (EoS).

**How to apply**:
- Fix a splice/run-on by: comma + coordinating conjunction; a semicolon; subordinating one clause; or splitting into two sentences (GoG).
- Very short, parallel clauses may take a comma (*Man proposes, God disposes*; *She leaned over to kiss him and he heard the rip*) (EoS, GoG).
- Don't break a sentence in two with a wrongful period (sentence fragment) unless the emphasis is deliberate (EoS; see SC8 for the intentional fragment).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Clark notes celebrated authors (DeLillo, McCarthy) win prizes with run-ons and comma splices — but "you run the risk of being punished if you use strategies for which privileged authors are rewarded," and only after you know the rule (see GP1).

**Sources**: EoS (II.5–6); SoS (Ch. 6, punctuation); GoG (Ch. 40).

<a id="gp6"></a>
### GP6 — Use the punctuation toolkit to control pace and emphasis

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Punctuation not controlling pace/emphasis; misused dash or semicolon  
**Fix** (act): Period for pace, dash/colon for emphasis, exclamation rarely.

**Principle**: Punctuation sets the reader's pace and marks syntax; "the main job of punctuation is to eliminate the ambiguities and garden paths" and "restore some of the prosody" (SoS). Treat each mark as a tool of meaning and effect (GoG).

**Why it matters**: Marks control rhythm and cinematic effect — Hillenbrand's *Seabiscuit* stretch run uses a comma-less run-on for flowing motion and seven semicolons for montage (GoG). Strings of modifiers without commas "progressively narrow the referent... like nested circles in a Venn diagram"; with commas they "just keep adding interesting facts" (SoS).

**How to apply** (synthesized from OWW, GoG, SoS):
- **Period**: reach it sooner than you think; break a mired long sentence into short ones — "there is no minimum length for a sentence."
- **Semicolon**: a "swinging gate" that connects and separates at once; use sparingly.
- **Dash**: one dash snaps emphasis at the end; a pair embeds a thought. "Do not use the dash because you have not mastered other forms of punctuation."
- **Colon**: introduce a list, statement, or quotation, or highlight an end word.
- **Exclamation point**: reserve it (Elmore Leonard: ~three per 100,000 words); don't use it to flag a joke — "humor is best achieved by understatement."
- **Ellipsis**: signals omission; "ellip responsibly" in reports; renders pauses in dialogue.
- **Question mark**: generates curiosity and narrative energy (see NS3).
- **Quotation marks**: periods/commas inside; mind scare quotes (VT6).

**Sources**: OWW (Ch. 10, Punctuation); GoG (Ch. 12, 14, 15, 17, 19, 20, 22); SoS (Ch. 6).

---

<a id="gp7"></a>
### GP7 — Place modifiers next to what they modify; avoid danglers

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Dangling or misplaced modifier  
**Fix** (act): Make the modifier's implied subject the clause subject, or recast (judgment).

**Principle**: "An introductory participial phrase must refer to the grammatical subject" (EoS): violations attach the modifier to the wrong noun and are "often ludicrous." Clark's rule: "Put things next to each other that belong together," or risk "contamination by proximity."

**Why it matters**: Misplaced modifiers hide in prose because "the writer's brain auto-corrects them," so they survive proofreading — and the result can be confusing or even libelous ("Reeling from accusations of child abuse, the minister attacked...") (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- *Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house cheap* → recast so the subject performs the action (EoS).
- Make the modifier's implied subject the explicit subject of the clause (Style); double-check opening and closing participles (GoG).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker insists most danglers "are not grammatical errors" but ambiguity risks; some are perfectly standard (*According to..., Considering..., To summarize...*). Whether to recast "is a matter of judgment, not grammar." (Connects to SC4's refinement: prevent wrong attachment.)

**Sources**: EoS (II.7); GoG (Ch. 26); Style (Lesson 9); SoS (Ch. 6).

---

<a id="gp8"></a>
### GP8 — Make subject and verb agree across intervening phrases

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Verb agrees with an intervening noun, not the true subject  
**Fix** (act): Strip the phrase to its head; agree with the real subject.

**Principle**: Singular subjects take singular verbs; plurals take plurals — but "the greater the distance between subject and verb, the more likely the writer will make a mistake" (GoG). Most grammar rules are "defined over trees, not strings," so when the field of vision "shrinks to a peephole," agreement, coordination, and case errors creep in (SoS, "tree-blindness").

**Why it matters**: *The bridge to the islands are crowded* happens because *islands* is "ringing in your mind's ear" rather than the true subject *bridge* (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Strip the phrase to its head and test: *the impact of the cuts → the impact... is* (SoS); *Her collection of Dalí's art... impresses* (GoG).
- For collective nouns (*posse, couple, collection*), decide whether the sense is singular or plural, or reword (GoG).
- For *who/whom*, move the word into its gap and try *he/him*; repair an unbalanced *both...and* so the phrases are parallel (SoS).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 24); SoS (Ch. 4, tree-blindness).

---

<a id="gp9"></a>
### GP9 — Honor real word-distinctions and confusables

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Confusables misused (fewer/less, disinterested, comprise); wrong fancy word  
**Fix** (act): Honor real distinctions; check a current usage guide for contested ones.

**Principle**: Keep the distinctions that careful readers notice: *fewer/less, flout/flaunt, disinterested/uninterested, infer/imply, compare to/compare with, militate/mitigate, credible/credulous* — and use a fancy word only in its true sense (*simplistic* ≠ *simple*) (EoS, OWW, SoS). Master *lie/lay*, *who/whom*, verb mood (the subjunctive for contrary-to-fact), and tense.

**Why it matters**: "Incorrect usage will lose you the readers you would most like to win" (OWW); precise distinctions "signal that you are sensitive to the shadings of usage." Using a fancy word as a wrong synonym "signals carelessness" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- *Lie* (recline; intransitive): lie/lay/lain. *Lay* (place; transitive): lay/laid/laid — "if the verb takes an object, use a form of *lay*" (GoG).
- Subjunctive for unreality: "If I *were* you..." (GoG).
- *while* should mean "during the time that"; *due to* relates to a noun, not an adverbial *because of*; *however* meaning "nevertheless" shouldn't lead its clause (EoS).
- Apply Bernstein's test for a new word: "Does it fill a real need?" (OWW).

**Nuances & disagreements**: Pinker (GP1) treats some traditional "distinctions" as myths (split infinitives, *that/which*), while keeping genuine ones (agreement, *who/whom* in formal prose, precise senses of confusables). Strunk's list is stricter than Pinker would endorse; check a current usage guide for contested items.

**Sources**: EoS (V); OWW (Usage); GoG (Ch. 23, 28, 29, 31, 35); SoS (Ch. 6).

<a id="gp10"></a>
### GP10 — Spell accurately; a misspelling is a speed bump

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=word · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Misspellings and homophone errors  
**Fix** (act): Use a dictionary, not just spell-check; keep a personal misspelling list.

**Principle**: Spell correctly to "convey the word you intend" and to "avoid distracting the reader." A single misplaced letter "can make all the difference" — *public/pubic, angel/angle* (GoG).

**Why it matters**: "Misspellings have the power to distract and confound." A spell-checker won't catch a correctly spelled wrong word (*angle* for *angel*) or homophones (*currant/current*).

**How to apply**:
- Don't rely on spell-checkers; use a dictionary.
- Keep a personal notebook of words you misspell; the act of listing helps you master them.
- Distinguish homonyms, homophones, and contranyms with care (GoG, Ch. 7).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 2).

## Narrative & Storytelling

<a id="ns1"></a>
### NS1 — Get people talking; find the human element; quote well

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Abstractions where people could speak; dead 'X said' lead-ins  
**Fix** (act): Quote real voices; lead with the quote; find the human element.

**Principle**: "Get people talking" — their own words "will always be better than your words" (OWW). For any drab subject, "look for the human element." When you quote, start the sentence with the quote, and "don't strain to find synonyms for 'he said.'"

**Why it matters**: Quotes carry inflection, regional flavor, and enthusiasm — "a person talking to the reader directly, not through the filter of a writer" (OWW). "Nothing is deader than to start a sentence with a 'Mr. Smith said' construction — it's where many readers stop reading." Readers "identify with people, not with abstractions."

**How to apply**:
- Lead with the quote, then break it once the reader knows who's talking (but not where it kills rhythm); don't quote anyone for more than three or four straight paragraphs (OWW).
- If you crave variety, use meaningful verbs (*pointed out, explained, replied*), not *expostulate* or "he smiled."
- Interview craft: prepare, take visible notes (not just tape), then "distill the essence"; the best material often comes "after you put your pencil away" (OWW).
- Find the human element even in science and sports — readers want "heroes who are believable," and "you are not the story" (OWW).

**Sources**: OWW (The Interview; Science; Sports).

---

<a id="ns2"></a>
### NS2 — Show with the telling detail and the power of names; pursue particularity

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): General claims without particulars; unnamed things  
**Fix** (act): Show the telling detail; name names; atomize evidence, use only what's needed.

**Principle**: "The particular separates the character from the crowd" (GoG): find "the small thing that represents the big thing." "Get the name of the dog and the brand and model of the bra"; "capital letters unleash the power of naming." A telling detail "obviates an explicit pronouncement" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "It is the specificity of the names that gives a paragraph authority" (GoG); proper names "bring the passage to life" and evoke a whole culture. A single great detail (the astronaut's diaper) can carry character and theme — and "leave room for the reader to draw conclusions."

**How to apply**:
- In research, "get the name" of everything — person, dog, flower, street (GoG); "know the names of things" (SSS).
- Follow general propositions with "specific, particular evidence" (GoG); show *and* tell, traveling the ladder of abstraction (see WC3).
- "Atomize your evidence" — dissect it into details and use "the one detail you need as you need it," in the order the prose needs, not the order you gathered it (SSS).

**Related**: WC3 (concrete over abstract); PR5 (let the fact land).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 21, 48); OWW (Trust Your Material); SoS (Ch. 1); SSS (know the names; atomize your evidence).

---

<a id="ns3"></a>
### NS3 — Drive narrative with a question or quest; tension pulls the reader along

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=draft · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Narrative without tension or a driving question  
**Fix** (act): Frame around a question/quest; after each line answer 'what next?'.

**Principle**: "It is the great question that gives energy to a narrative" (GoG); the best stories "are formed around a question that the story answers for the reader." "Any time you can tell a story in the form of a quest or a pilgrimage you'll be ahead of the game" (OWW). Narrative is "the oldest and most compelling method of holding someone's attention."

**Why it matters**: A driving question acts as "a little promise from the author" — Nazario's "Would he make it?" pulls readers through 300 pages (GoG). "Logic is the glue... tension must be maintained from one sentence to the next... narrative is what should pull your readers along without their noticing the tug" (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Frame a piece around a question the reader wants answered; after each sentence ask "What does the reader want to know next?" and answer it (OWW).
- Use direct and rhetorical questions to raise the dramatic pitch (AS; see SC8).
- Let readers' own associations do work — allusion and resonance "can do a great deal of emotional work that writers can't achieve on their own" (OWW).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 18); OWW (The Lead; A Writer's Decisions); AS (Ch. 11).

---

<a id="ns4"></a>
### NS4 — Use figurative language sparingly and accurately; avoid clichés and mixed metaphors

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=revise · level=sentence · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Cliched, mixed, or over-extended metaphors  
**Fix** (act): Use fresh figures sparingly; ensure the comparison is literally accurate.

**Principle**: Develop "an associative imagination" with simile, metaphor, and analogy — "the metaphor asserts more power than the simile because the author closes the distance" (GoG). But "avoid clichés like the plague" (SoS), and use a true metaphor rarely: it should "expire in a sudden flash of light," requiring "a correspondence that must be literally accurate" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: When a reader works "through one stale idiom after another, she stops converting the language into mental images"; a cliché-monger "will inevitably mix his metaphors" (SoS). Clichés are "dead matter... the debris of someone else's thinking," and "you can't fix a cliché by using it ironically" (SSS). Per Frye, in any comparison "the differences are as important as the resemblances" (GoG).

**How to apply**:
- Reach for "fresh similes and metaphors that keep the reader's sensory cortexes lit up" (SoS); a man "with such long legs that he looked like the afternoon shadow of somebody else" (Dickens).
- If you must use a shopworn image, word it so it makes physical sense (*fall through the cracks*, not *between* them); look up a cliché's livelier original (SoS).
- Avoid the "extended metaphor... a cruel analogical death"; the less you use the device, the more effective it is (SSS).
- Use analogy to explain the less-known by the more-known (GoG).

**Related**: WC5 (shun clichés/journalese); WC3 (concrete imagery).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 44–45); SoS (Ch. 2); SSS (true metaphor); OWW (clichés/journalese).

---

<a id="ns5"></a>
### NS5 — Render dialect and slang with a light, accurate touch

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Heavy phonetic dialect; only 'colorful' characters in dialect  
**Fix** (act): Use minimum deviation; render via vocabulary/syntax; avoid stereotype.

**Principle**: Render dialect through sound, vocabulary, and syntax, but follow E. B. White: "Do not use dialect unless your ear is good," and "use the minimum, not the maximum, of deviation from the norm" (GoG). Slang gives access to a group's voice and "defines a group," but much of it is "short-lived."

**Why it matters**: "There is nothing inherent in a dialect that makes it superior or inferior," yet it provokes prejudice; writers err by rendering only "colorful" characters in dialect while putting others in standard prose (GoG, Frady's Wallace). Slang and a "discourse community's" lingo, gathered well, convey authenticity (see WC5).

**How to apply**:
- Capture authenticity through all three elements, sparingly, and avoid crossing into stereotype (GoG).
- Tame taboo language for genuine purposes — realistic speech, a single shocking blow, defining character — since overuse deadens it (GoG).

**Sources**: GoG (Ch. 41–43).

## Persuasion & Rhetoric

<a id="pr1"></a>
### PR1 — Style is an ethical choice: write honestly; write to others as you'd have them write to you

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Agency hidden to evade responsibility; needless difficulty  
**Fix** (act): Write honestly; name responsible agents; make it no harder than it must be.

**Principle**: "Style is just the polish" is wrong: a choice of subjects and verbs "has an ethical dimension" (Style). The First Rule of Ethical Writing: "Write to others as you would have others write to you" — make ideas "no simpler than they deserve, but no more difficult than they have to be." Style "earns trust" (SoS); writing is "a public trust" (OWW).

**Why it matters**: Readers infer an *ethos* from your prose that "hardens into our reputation" (Style); a writer careful in prose is assumed careful in conduct (SoS). Writing as we wouldn't want written to us "abrades the trust that sustains a civil society" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Beware **intended misdirection** — hiding the responsible agent in passives, nominalizations, and agentless verbs (Sears's "mistakes may have occurred"; the recall letter that made the car, not the driver, the topic) (Style). Distinguish this from the kind impulse to soften bad news.
- In quoting, follow "brevity and fair play" — select and tighten, but never fabricate or quote out of context (OWW).
- Don't rationalize opacity: that ideas are too new for clarity is "more often wrong than right" (Style).

**Sources**: Style (Lesson 11); OWW (editing quotes); SoS (Prologue).

---

<a id="pr2"></a>
### PR2 — Keep proportion; give substantial counterarguments their own space

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Verbiage lopsided vs. the argument; counterpoints intruding piecemeal  
**Fix** (act): Match coverage to centrality; give counterarguments their own section.

**Principle**: "The amount of verbiage one devotes to a point should not be too far out of line with how central it is to the argument" (SoS). Give significant counterarguments "a section of their own."

**Why it matters**: When most of the verbiage pushes one way while the author argues the other, "the reader gets the feeling that he's being bullied rather than persuaded" (SoS, Keegan).

**How to apply**: Don't let counterexamples "repeatedly intrude into the main line of an argument while browbeating readers into looking away" — handle them in one place (SoS).

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 5).

---

<a id="pr3"></a>
### PR3 — Take a clear stand; express opinion firmly

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Hedged, evasive conclusion  
**Fix** (act): Take a clear stand; don't cancel it with last-minute escapes.

**Principle**: Columns, editorials, and criticism "consist of personal opinion — express your opinion firmly. Don't cancel its strength with last-minute evasions and escapes" (OWW).

**Why it matters**: "The most boring sentence in the daily newspaper is the last sentence of the editorial," which hedges ("It is too early to tell..."). "If it's too early to tell, don't bother us with it" (OWW).

**How to apply**: "Take your stand with conviction" (OWW). (Reinforces VT3 on confidence; for critics: love the medium, use specific detail, avoid ecstatic adjectives, and don't give away the plot.)

**Sources**: OWW (Critics and Columnists).

---

<a id="pr4"></a>
### PR4 — Humor is a secret weapon: heighten a crazy truth

**Type**: Framework  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Flat where humor could heighten a truth (craft)  
**Fix** (act): Heighten a real absurdity via a controlled comic device; joke once.

**Principle**: "Humor is the secret weapon of the nonfiction writer" — and "if you're trying to write humor, almost everything you do is serious" (OWW). The essence is "heightening of some crazy truth to a level where it will be seen as crazy," via a comic device (satire, parody, irony).

**Why it matters**: "One strong editorial cartoon is worth a hundred solemn editorials"; humor jolts readers into seeing the routine-but-outlandish freshly. "Most humor is based on fundamental truths," so start from truth, not invention (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Control is vital: stick close to the form you're parodying; make a joke only once; "don't strain for laughs — humor is built on surprise"; cut "the extra sentence that explains a funny point already implicit" (OWW).
- Master straight English first; trust the sophistication of readers.

**Sources**: OWW (Humor).

---

<a id="pr5"></a>
### PR5 — Let allusion, resonance, and the unadorned fact do the persuading

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Over-explained facts; emotion told, not evoked  
**Fix** (act): Use allusion/resonance; let a striking fact speak for itself.

**Principle**: Tap "associations that readers bring to the story" — allusion and resonance — and let a startling fact "speak for itself: just the facts, please" (OWW). "Trust your material... there's nothing more interesting than the truth."

**Why it matters**: "The reader plays a major role in the act of writing and must be given room to play it" (OWW). An exclamation point or an over-explanation on an amazing fact "would have spoiled [readers'] own pleasure of discovery." Pre-judging a fact with *surprisingly, of course* robs the reader of marveling.

**How to apply**:
- Present the self-sufficient striking fact and let readers do the marveling (OWW: "We were in Timbuktu for an event that hadn't been held in 27 years," left without comment).
- Use eloquence that runs on "what it leaves unsaid, touching off echoes" in the reader's heritage (OWW).

**Related**: VT6 (restraint; don't overstate); NS2 (telling detail).

**Sources**: OWW (Trust Your Material; A Writer's Decisions; The Sound of Your Voice).

---

<a id="pr6"></a>
### PR6 — Writing attests and witnesses more than it proves

**Type**: Principle (counter-view)  
**Tags**: tasks=both · phase=both · level=document · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Over-reliance on logical scaffolding (stance)  
**Fix** (act): Favor testimony/witness; connect by relevance, not forced logic. (Genre-sensitive.)

**Principle**: "There's little actual logic in good writing... Writing doesn't prove anything, and it only rarely persuades. It does something much better. It attests. It witnesses... Proof is for mathematicians. Logic is for philosophers. We have testimony" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: "What passes for logic or argument is usually little more than a succession of ideas connected mostly by proximity and analogy." "Don't confuse order with linearity" — order means "merely connections, some close, some oblique."

**How to apply**: Cut the logical indicators that "insist upon logic whether it exists or not" (see SO9); aim to share "your interest in what you've noticed" rather than to browbeat.

**Nuances & disagreements**: This is a deliberate counterweight to the argument-and-signposting emphasis of Style and SoS (SO7, SO9, PR1–PR2). Klinkenborg distrusts the apparatus of argument; Williams and Pinker treat clear argument structure as a service to the reader. Useful framing: in expository/persuasive work, lean toward Williams/Pinker; in literary nonfiction and the personal essay, Klinkenborg's "testimony" model often fits better.

**Sources**: SSS (writing doesn't prove; cut the logical indicators).

## Process, Revision & Editing

<a id="pro1"></a>
### PRO1 — Rewriting is the essence; the rules are for revising, not drafting

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): First-draft prose treated as final  
**Fix** (act): Revise relentlessly toward brevity, clarity, rhythm; the rules are for revising.

**Principle**: "Rewriting is the essence of writing well: it's where the game is won or lost" (OWW). Williams's principles "have little to do with how you draft, much to do with how you revise" — thinking about them while drafting risks "paralysis." Klinkenborg goes furthest: "All writing is revision... Revise at the point of composition. Accept no provisional sentences."

**Why it matters**: "The newly hatched sentence almost always has something wrong with it" (OWW); writing is "an evolving process, not a product." "Perfection may be the ideal, but it is the death of done" (Style).

**How to apply**:
- Most rewriting is "reshaping and tightening," not new drafts (OWW); make small, incremental changes at the sentence level, with no priority to beginning or end (SSS).
- "You can almost never fix a sentence by using only the words it already contains" — reimagine it; "the simplest revision is deletion" (SSS).
- Klinkenborg's revision compass — revise *toward* brevity, directness, simplicity, clarity, rhythm, literalness, implication, variation, silence, and presence; "when things are really working... break what already works."

**Related**: PRO6 (techniques); PRO3 (read aloud); CC1 (omit needless words).

**Sources**: OWW (Rewriting); Style (Lesson 1; "death of done"); SSS (all writing is revision; can't fix with the same words; what to revise toward).

---

<a id="pro2"></a>
### PRO2 — Beat the curse of knowledge: get feedback and become a stranger to your work

**Type**: Rule  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P1  
**Signal** (detect): Writer assumes the reader knows what the writer knows  
**Fix** (act): Show drafts to real readers; reread cold; assume readers know less than you.

**Principle**: The **curse of knowledge** — "a difficulty in imagining what it is like for someone else not to know something that you know" — is "the single best explanation of why good people write bad prose" (SoS). The cure is to "close the loop": show a draft to readers like your audience, and "show a draft to yourself" after time passes. "None of us can judge our own writing as others will" (Style).

**Why it matters**: "The better you know something, the less you remember how hard it was to learn"; "we do not notice the curse because the curse prevents us from noticing it" (SoS). Just trying harder to imagine the reader "doesn't make you a whole lot more accurate" — "often it's enough that [your reviewers] are not you."

**How to apply**:
- Assume readers are "as intelligent and sophisticated as you are, but happen not to know something you know"; "it's wiser to assume too little than too much," without condescending (SoS).
- Don't implement every suggestion ("good prose is never written by a committee"), but revise when feedback recurs or makes sense to you (SoS).
- "One basic strategy for revision is becoming a stranger to what you've written" (SSS); be your own first reader, without "the immense foreknowledge of having written it."

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 3); Style (Lesson 1); SSS (becoming a stranger; be your own reader).

---

<a id="pro3"></a>
### PRO3 — Read your work aloud

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Prose only ever read silently  
**Fix** (act): Read aloud to catch rhythm, repetition, garden paths, weak endings.

**Principle**: "Read everything aloud before letting it go out" (OWW). "The ear is much smarter than the eye... because the eye can't see rhythm or hear unwanted repetition" (SSS). Reading aloud also catches garden paths — most "exist only on the printed page" (SoS).

**Why it matters**: "How well you read aloud reveals how well you understand the syntax of a sentence" (SSS). Tapping hard on a sentence's last words reveals whether the stress falls on words that deserve it (Style). "If you stumble as you recite a sentence, it may mean you're tripping on your own treacherous syntax" (SoS).

**How to apply**:
- Read for rhythm "without overemphasizing it," so a listener can hear "the shape of the syntax" (SSS).
- Listen for clichés — "notice how incriminating they sound" (OWW); read poems aloud to learn rhythm.

**Related**: PRO6 (self-editing); SC2 (stress position); WC7 (sound).

**Sources**: OWW (Words; Rewriting); SSS (read aloud; the inner quaver); SoS (Ch. 4); Style (Lesson 5).

---

<a id="pro4"></a>
### PRO4 — Become a good reader; learn by imitation and reverse-engineering

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): Thin repertoire of models  
**Fix** (act): Read widely; reverse-engineer and imitate strong models.

**Principle**: "The starting point for becoming a good writer is to be a good reader. Writers acquire their technique by spotting, savoring, and reverse-engineering examples of good prose" (SoS). "Writing is learned by imitation," so "cultivate the best models" (OWW). "You can only become a better writer by becoming a better reader" (SSS).

**Why it matters**: The writer's "ear" is "the tacit sense of style" absorbed from "a vast inventory of words, idioms, constructions, tropes" (SoS). Imitating sloppy sources breeds clichés; imitating strong ones builds a repertoire (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Read like a writer: see prose as "the living tissue of a writer's choices," and ask why each word, rhythm, and shape was chosen — even mark up a master's page by parts of speech and devices (SSS).
- "Never hesitate to imitate another writer" with taste — "get their voice and their taste into your ear"; reread *The Elements of Style* once a year (OWW).
- Reading is "extraction" is the wrong model — attend to "the very sentence, word for word," not just its meaning (SSS).

**Sources**: SoS (Ch. 1); OWW (Words; Sound of Your Voice); SSS (reading like a writer; analyze a master's prose).

<a id="pro5"></a>
### PRO5 — Writing is hard craft, not a gift or "flow"; focus on process and keep at it

**Type**: Guideline  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Waiting for 'flow'/inspiration; feeling blocked  
**Fix** (act): Treat writing as hard craft; write on a schedule anywhere; focus on process.

**Principle**: "Writing is hard work. A clear sentence is no accident" (OWW); writing is "a craft with its own tools, not a talent some are born with." "Flow is something the reader experiences, not the writer" (SSS) — "writing doesn't flow, unless you're plagiarizing or collecting clichés." Concentrate on the process: "if the process is sound, the product will take care of itself" (OWW).

**Why it matters**: Believing writing should flow but finding it doesn't makes writers feel "blocked"; "the idea of writer's block exists largely because of the notion that writing should flow" (SSS). "If you find that writing is hard, it's because it is hard" (OWW) — "your labor isn't a sign of defeat. It's a sign of engagement."

**How to apply**:
- "You learn to write by writing," on a regular schedule (OWW); "learn to write anywhere, at any time, in any conditions" — every required ritual "becomes a prohibition when it's lacking" (SSS).
- Distrust the myths of "genius," "inspiration," "natural," and "flow" (SSS). Talking is natural; writing is "an unnatural act" (SoS) that "takes years of work."
- Keep enjoyment and curiosity alive: "writers who write interestingly... keep themselves interested" (OWW); discipline "is nothing more than interest and expectation" (SSS).

**Sources**: OWW (Simplicity; Unity; The Tyranny of the Final Product; Enjoyment, Fear and Confidence); SSS (flow; "natural"; discipline); SoS (Ch. 2).

<a id="pro6"></a>
### PRO6 — Use concrete self-editing techniques

**Type**: Framework  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P2  
**Signal** (detect): No systematic editing pass  
**Fix** (act): Bracket/delete tests; underline sentence openings; ask 'do I need it?'.

**Principle**: Make revision mechanical where you can. **Bracket** every component not doing useful work, then read the sentence without it (OWW). **Underline** the first seven or eight words of each sentence to expose abstract subjects and missing characters (Style). When stuck on a troublesome phrase, ask "Do I need it at all?" — "probably you don't" (OWW). **Turn every sentence into its own paragraph** to see length and shape, then interrogate each (SSS).

**Why it matters**: These give the reader's-eye view that the curse of knowledge denies (PRO2), and turn vague advice into repeatable diagnostics. "Most first drafts can be cut by 50 percent without losing any information or the author's voice" (OWW).

**How to apply** — Klinkenborg's per-sentence interrogation: How many begin with the subject? Are nouns multisyllabic Latinate *-ion* words or "the solid names of actual things"? How close is each subject to its verb? How many verbs are forms of *to be*; active or passive? Can the sentence be broken in two? Are there opening modifiers that don't modify the subject? — Then trust "the inner quaver," the "barely discernible tremor" that signals a problem, and "find out what's causing it and fix it."

**Related**: CC1 (omit needless words); WC2 (diagnose nominalizations); SO4 (underline openings for coherence).

**Sources**: OWW (Clutter; The Quickest Fix); Style (Lessons 2 & 4); SSS (turn every sentence into a paragraph; the inner quaver).

<a id="pro7"></a>
### PRO7 — Gather more material than you use, then select ruthlessly

**Type**: Tip  
**Tags**: tasks=create · phase=both · level=process · priority=P3  
**Signal** (detect): Too little material, or hoarding the best for last  
**Fix** (act): Collect a surplus, then select; don't ration -- use the best where it serves.

**Principle**: "You should always collect more material than you will use. Every article is strong in proportion to the surplus of details from which you can choose" — but "at some point you must stop researching and start writing" (OWW).

**Why it matters**: A surplus lets you pick the most telling details (see NS2); "always start with too much material, then give your reader just enough" (OWW).

**How to apply**:
- Look beyond obvious sources — signs, labels, menus, obscure corners — for a lead "different from everybody else's" (OWW).
- Take notes "on what interests you," but don't turn them into an outline (SSS; see SO11); then "think, and think again. Learn to be patient in the presence of your thoughts."

**Nuances & disagreements**: A subtle tension on *using* the material. Zinsser frames selection as choosing "the few [details] that will serve you best." Klinkenborg says "squander your material — don't ration it, saving the best for last. You don't know what the best is. Use it up." Both reject hoarding; they differ on whether to select sparingly (OWW) or pour it out and discover value in the act (SSS).

**Sources**: OWW (The Lead; Science); SSS (squander your material; research and take notes).
