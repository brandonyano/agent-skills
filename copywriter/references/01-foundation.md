## 1. Market Awareness Levels

---

### Schwartz's Five Stages of Market Awareness

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Every market passes through five stages of awareness, and your headline must match your market's current stage. This determines not just what you say, but HOW you say it:

**Stage 1 - Most Aware:**
Customer knows your product, knows what it does, knows he wants it. Just hasn't bought yet. These are repeat buyers, loyal fans who trust your brand.
*Headline approach: Name product + bargain price. Cut to the deal quickly.*

**Stage 2 - Product Aware:**
Customer knows your product but isn't sure it's right for them. Not convinced of how well it works. They need trust-building more than education.
*Headline approach: Product name + superiority claim. Focus heavily on proof and credibility.*

**Stage 3 - Solution Aware:**
Customer knows the result they want but doesn't know your product provides it. They have only an outcome in mind.
*Headline approach: Name desire/solution + prove it can be accomplished + show mechanism in product.*

**Stage 4 - Problem Aware:**
Customer recognizes he has a problem but doesn't know there's a solution. They have worry but not hope.
*Headline approach: Name the problem/need + crystallize it + present solution. Say "I sympathize" before naming benefits.*

**Stage 5 - Completely Unaware:**
Customer is not aware of desire, need, or won't admit it without being led. They have no reason to trust or listen to your message.
*Headline approach: Identification, echo emotion/attitude, call market together. Never start with product mentions.*

**Why It Matters**: 
A headline that works in one stage will fail completely in another. The awareness level determines which lead type will work best—more aware customers respond to direct leads; less aware customers need indirect leads. Using Stage 1 headlines (price/product) on Stage 5 markets (completely unaware) is a recipe for failure.

**How to Implement**: 
- Before writing, plot your prospect on the awareness scale
- Ask: "What does my customer already know about me, my product, and their own problems?"
- Research where your market currently sits
- Write headlines that meet prospects at their exact awareness level
- Move prospects from their current stage toward purchase through progressive copy

**Examples**:
- **Stage 1 (Most Aware)**: "Revere Zomar Lens, Electric Eye Camera—Formerly $149.50—Now Only $119.95"
- **Stage 2 (Product Aware)**: "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise in a Rolls Royce is the electric clock"
- **Stage 3 (Solution Aware)**: "How to win friends and influence people" / "If you're feeling run down, then this will pick you up" (Geritol)
- **Stage 4 (Problem Aware)**: "Corns?" / "Shrinks hemorrhoids without surgery" / "Five Familiar Skin Troubles — Which Do You Want to Overcome?"
- **Stage 5 (Completely Unaware)**: "They laughed when I sat down at the piano—But when I started to play!" / Wall Street Journal's "Tale of Two Young Men"
- **Case Study**: Apple "fanboys" who camp outside stores represent Most Aware customers—they just need the deal
- **Case Study**: Kleenex pivoted from "cold cream towel replacement" to "Don't Put a Cold In Your Pocket" by recognizing they needed to address the actual problem (Stage 4)

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Using Stage 1 headlines (price/product) on Stage 5 markets (completely unaware)
- Mentioning product in headline when market doesn't know it exists
- Direct claims to markets that won't believe them yet
- Using the same lead approach for all awareness levels
- Assuming prospects know more than they do
- Being too direct with unaware prospects
- Jumping straight to product features with Solution-Aware prospects

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz, Great Leads - Masterson & Forde

### The Five Stages of Market Sophistication

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Markets also pass through five stages of SOPHISTICATION—how many similar products and promises they've been exposed to:

**Stage 1 - First in Market:**
Be simple, direct, don't be fancy. Name need or claim—nothing more. Being first is the dream of every copywriter. It happens through technological breakthroughs, insight into new applications, new media, or discovering previously unnoticed performances.

**Stage 2 - Second in Market:**
Copy the successful claim but ENLARGE it. Drive to absolute limit. Outbid competition. When claims still have power, the competitor with the biggest claim wins.

**Stage 3 - Exhausted Direct Claims:**
Introduce NEW MECHANISM to make old claims fresh and believable again.

**Stage 4 - Exhausted Mechanism:**
ELABORATE or ENLARGE the successful mechanism.

**Stage 5 - Completely Exhausted:**
Shift to IDENTIFICATION. Bring prospect into ad through identity, not through desire.

**Why It Matters**: 
Sophistication determines whether direct claims still work or whether you need fresh mechanisms or identification approaches. Using Stage 1 tactics in Stage 5 markets is ineffective.

**How to Implement**: 
- Research competing ads in your field
- Determine which stage the market has reached
- Match your approach to the current sophistication level
- Be first with new mechanisms when possible

**Examples**:
- **Stage 1**: "NOW! LOSE UGLY FAT!" (first in market)
- **Stage 2**: "LOSE UP TO 47 POUNDS IN 4 WEEKS—OR RECEIVE $40 BACK!" (enlarged claim)
- **Stage 3**: "FLOATS FAT RIGHT OUT OF YOUR BODY!" (new mechanism)
- **Stage 4**: "FIRST NO-DIET REDUCING WONDER DRUG!" (elaborated mechanism)
- **Stage 5**: "WHY MEN CRACK..." (identification)
- **Technological Breakthrough Example**: Women's hair sprays, long-playing records
- **New Application Example**: Ovaltine switched from insomnia aid to body builder for skinny children
- **New Media Example**: Revlon's sponsorship of "The $64,000 Question"
- **New Performance Example**: Lifebuoy discovered perspiration odor angle and created "B.O."

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Using Stage 1 tactics in Stage 5 markets
- Failing to recognize when mechanisms have lost believability
- Not introducing fresh mechanisms when claims are exhausted
- Over-complicating simple first-in-market opportunities
- Not capitalizing quickly enough on first-mover advantage

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz

### The "Hyper-Aware" Exception: When High Awareness Is Negative

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Occasionally, high awareness is negative—"hyper-aware" prospects who have been hit too many times with similar pitches, tried products that let them down, or reached spending limits. They need indirect approaches to rebuild trust.

**Why It Matters**: 
Not all awareness is positive. Jaded customers who've "heard it all before" won't respond to direct approaches even though they know your product. Sophisticated buyers recognize influence attempts and resist them.

**How to Implement**: 
- Recognize when high awareness equals high skepticism
- Move toward indirect lead types (Story or Proclamation leads)
- Rebuild trust before making offers
- Find new angles they haven't heard
- Don't trigger "stomach tightening" warning signals
- Avoid techniques that feel obviously manipulative
- Keep the "autopilot" engaged rather than activating conscious resistance

**Examples**:
- **Situation**: Car lot customers (aware but resistant)
- **Situation**: Over-marketed niches with promise fatigue
- **Solution**: Use Story or Proclamation leads to sidestep resistance
- **Case Study**: Cialdini's personal defense against consistency traps—asking "Would I make this choice again knowing what I know now?"

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Assuming all aware customers are ready to buy
- Using direct leads on jaded audiences
- Ignoring accumulated skepticism
- Techniques so obvious they trigger conscious resistance
- Pressure so heavy it activates defensive scrutiny

**Sources**: Great Leads - Masterson & Forde, Influence - Robert Cialdini

### The 19.5x Difference: Why Appeals Matter More Than Execution

**Type**: Metric

**Principle**: 
One advertisement can sell 19.5 times as much as another, even when both occupy the same space, run in the same publication, have photographic illustrations, and carefully written copy. The difference is using the right appeal vs. the wrong appeal. There is no "neutral" advertising—bad advertising is worse than no advertising because it can unsell your product.

**Why It Matters**: 
The magnitude of difference between advertisements is enormous. Getting the appeal right is everything. Focusing on execution over strategy misses the main point.

**How to Implement**: 
- Test different appeals before committing budget
- Focus on finding the RIGHT promise, not just A promise
- Use research to identify consumer hot buttons
- Test advertising before full rollout
- Measure results rigorously
- Be willing to kill campaigns that don't perform

**Examples**:
- **Case Study (Caples)**: John Caples documented one ad selling 19.5x more than another with identical space, publication, and visual treatment
- **Case Study (Ford)**: George Hay Brown at Ford inserted ads in every other copy of Reader's Digest. After a year, people NOT exposed to advertising had bought MORE Fords than those who had been exposed
- **Case Study (Beer)**: Consumption of a certain brand of beer was lower among people who remembered its advertising than those who did not—the brewer had spent millions on advertising that "un-sold his beer"

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Treating all ads as roughly equivalent
- Not testing appeals before launch
- Focusing on execution over strategy
- Assuming all advertising helps to some degree
- Running campaigns without measuring results
- Continuing ineffective campaigns due to sunk cost fallacy

**Sources**: Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy (citing John Caples)

## 2. Customer Research Methods

---

### The "Starving Crowd" Principle: Market Demand Trumps Everything

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
The single most important advantage in marketing is having a "starving crowd"—a group of people desperately hungry for what you're selling. Market selection trumps everything else including product quality, location, and price. A person who has uncovered a hot market can always find a product to fill the needs of that market, but a person with a product cannot always find a hot market for it.

**Why It Matters**: 
You can have the best hamburger in the world, but a competitor with a mediocre product selling to hungry people will outsell you every time. Market demand is the foundation of all successful marketing. Brilliant copy about a weak product equals nothing; mediocre copy about a desired product equals a torrent of sales.

**How to Implement**: 
- Before creating any product, identify markets with intense, unfulfilled desire
- Look for people actively spending money to solve problems
- Study what people actually buy, not what they say they would buy
- Use list data (SRDS Direct Mail List Book) to identify hot markets by list size and activity
- Evaluate product strength honestly
- Recognize when timing is right or wrong for a product

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "I want to find people who have already spent $5,000 on diamonds and sell them more diamonds"
- **Bad**: "I invented this great product, now let me find someone to buy it"
- **Case Study (Halbert)**: Gary's hamburger stand analogy—given the choice of any advantage (better meat, better buns, better location), he would choose only "a starving crowd" because it guarantees sales
- **Case Study (Halbert)**: Gary discovered a list of pregnant mothers (120,000 new names monthly) and created "How To Raise Your Child's IQ Before It Is Even Born" specifically for that market
- **Case Study (Pringles vs Bell Brand)**: Bell Brand potato chips defeated P&G's Pringles because they tasted better—advertising couldn't save an inferior product

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Creating a product first, then looking for a market (this is "backasswards")
- Assuming your product is so good people will automatically want it
- Trusting surveys about what people say they want instead of observing actual behavior
- Expecting copy to overcome fundamental product weaknesses
- Not recognizing when timing is wrong for a product

**Sources**: The Boron Letters - Gary Halbert, How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab, Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy

### Mass Desire: Copy Cannot Create, Only Channel

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears, and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product. A market is born at the moment when a private desire is shared by a statistically significant number of people, large enough to profitably repay selling to them.

**Why It Matters**: 
Attempting to create desire rather than channel it is ineffective because it requires education rather than advertising. Education produces at best one dollar in sales for every dollar spent—advertising that channels existing desire produces $50 to $100 for every dollar spent (the Amplification Effect).

**How to Implement**: 
- Study society to detect existing mass desires
- Inventory and chart their force and direction
- Never try to create new desires—harness existing ones
- Focus on directing, not creating, consumer motivation
- Calculate if the market size justifies advertising costs
- Monitor social, economic, and technological forces that create desires

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "Light a Lucky, and you'll never miss sweets that make you fat" (channels existing desire to lose weight onto cigarettes)
- **Bad (Chrysler 1948)**: Tried to sell functional cars when market wanted longer, lower, wider cars (fought against mass desire)
- **Case Study (Ford 1954)**: Ford safety campaign failed because it tried to create desire for safety when market wanted horsepower. Next year Ford produced highest-horsepower engines in history.

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Trying to create desire where none exists
- Fighting against established trends
- Spending budget on education rather than amplification

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz, The Copywriter's Handbook - Robert Bly

### The Three Dimensions of Mass Desire: Urgency, Staying Power, Scope

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Every mass desire has three vital dimensions you must evaluate:
1. **Urgency** - Intensity, degree of demand to be satisfied
2. **Staying Power** - Degree of repetition, inability to become satiated
3. **Scope** - Number of people who share this desire

Choosing which desire to feature in your headline is the most important step in writing your ad. If wrong, nothing else matters.

**Why It Matters**: 
These dimensions help you rate potential desires and choose the one with maximum sales power. High urgency creates immediate action; high staying power creates repeat purchases; high scope creates large markets.

**How to Implement**: 
- Rate potential desires on all three dimensions
- Choose the desire with maximum power across all three
- Feature only ONE predominant desire in your headline
- Use secondary desires to reinforce in body copy

**Examples**:
- **High Urgency**: Constant arthritic pain vs. minor headache
- **High Staying Power**: Raw hunger vs. craving for gourmet foods
- **High Scope**: Gas savings vs. preventing future repair bills
- **Excellent Market**: Women wanting to lose weight (massive, ongoing, insatiable market)

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Trying to address multiple desires equally
- Choosing low-urgency desires over high-urgency ones
- Ignoring staying power (leads to one-time purchases only)

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz

### The Two Forces Creating Mass Desire: Permanent vs. Changing

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Mass desires are created by two categories of forces:

**1. Permanent Forces:**
- Mass Instinct (women wanting to be attractive, men wanting to be virile, health desires)
- Mass Technological Problems (bad TV reception, corroding mufflers, slow pain relief)

**2. Forces of Change:**
- Trends (beginning, fulfillment, reversal)
- Style shifts
- Mass Education (society trickle-down, advertising cumulative effect)

**Why It Matters**: 
The type of force determines your copywriting strategy—permanent forces require differentiation; changing forces require timing and sensitivity.

**How to Implement**: 
- For permanent forces: Create fresh appeals, build stronger believability, shift desire from competitors
- For forces of change: Develop sensitivity, foresight, intuition; be first to catch rising tides
- Monitor when to shift appeals as trends change

**Examples**:
- **Permanent**: Weight loss desire (never fades, continuous market renewal)
- **Force of Change**: Horsepower appeal in the 50s suddenly subordinated to gas economy

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Treating changing forces as permanent
- Missing timing on trend reversals
- Not adapting to new mass education effects

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz

### Watch What People DO, Not What They SAY

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
To deduce what people want to buy, observe what they actually DO buy—not what they say they buy or think they should buy. People's wallets reveal their true desires; their mouths often lie. Marketing decisions based on stated preferences lead to expensive failures, while decisions based on actual purchasing behavior lead to profits.

**Why It Matters**: 
People habitually give the "right" answer rather than the truthful one. Customers in focus groups are being paid and perform accordingly. Real online conversations reveal authentic opinions and language.

**How to Implement**: 
- Study sales figures, not survey results
- Look at what products people repeatedly purchase
- Track where people actually spend their money
- Be skeptical of market research based on questionnaires
- Observe behavior patterns, not stated intentions
- Monitor social media conversations about your product
- Read Amazon reviews and forum discussions
- Look for the language customers actually use

**Examples**:
- **Good**: A beer company discovering through sales data that customers buy regular beer, despite claiming to prefer premium beer in surveys
- **Bad**: Basing a product launch on survey data showing people prefer premium products
- **Case Study (National Enquirer)**: The largest selling newspaper in the world, yet almost nobody admits to reading it. "It must be all those Martians."
- **Case Study (Mail Order)**: People say "I never buy anything by mail" yet mail order sales are in the billions

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Trusting what people say in focus groups
- Believing survey data over transaction data
- Assuming stated preferences match actual behavior
- Relying on people paid $50 and a Diet Dr. Pepper to critique your work
- Treating focus group feedback as gospel
- Ignoring freely available online feedback

**Sources**: The Boron Letters - Gary Halbert, Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

### "Enter the Conversation Already Happening in Their Mind"

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Before writing any sales letter, understand that your reader is constantly engaged in a mental conversation with himself about his own interests, desires, and problems. Your letter must join that conversation, not interrupt it with your own agenda. Every reader has an "It's-me!" moment where they recognize themselves in your writing—your job is to create that recognition as quickly as possible.

**Why It Matters**: 
This is Robert Collier's most famous principle. When you align with what's already on your reader's mind, you have instant rapport. When you interrupt their mental conversation to talk about yourself, you're immediately rejected—just as you would reject a stranger who slapped you on the back mid-conversation to sell you something. Readers are scanning to determine if content is relevant to them; the faster they see themselves in your writing, the more likely they are to keep reading and take action.

**How to Implement**: 
- Research what your target audience thinks about constantly
- Start your letter with something that relates to their ongoing concerns
- Bridge from their interest to your offer naturally
- Never open with your product or company—open with THEM
- Open with a scenario or problem your specific reader experiences
- Use "you" language throughout
- Reference specific situations, roles, or challenges your reader faces
- Mirror the reader's language and terminology back to them

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "After baby's food and baby's clothes, the most important thing you have to decide upon is the little cart baby is going to ride in..." (To a new mother)
- **Good**: "Your boy is a little shaver now. He thinks you are the most wonderful man in the world..." (To a father)
- **Good**: "If you've ever stared at a blank page wondering what to write in your next email..."
- **Bad**: "Our company has been in business for 25 years and we'd like to tell you about our wonderful product..."
- **Bad**: "Email marketing is an important component of any integrated digital strategy..."
- **Case Study**: This principle was the foundation for Collier selling over $2,000,000 worth of books

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Starting letters with "I" or company name
- Opening with product features instead of reader concerns
- Assuming the reader cares about your business
- Interrupting rather than joining the mental conversation
- Opening with company history or credentials instead of reader relevance
- Using "we" when you should be using "you"
- Being too generic to create specific recognition

**Sources**: The Robert Collier Letter Book - Robert Collier, Everybody Writes - Ann Handley

### Study Reader First, Product Second

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
"Study your reader first—your product second. If you understand his reactions, and present those phases of your product that relate to his needs, then you cannot help but write a good letter." The very first thing you must realize is that you must become a "student of markets"—not products, not techniques, not copywriting, not media buying.

**Why It Matters**: 
Familiarity with your product is an advantage, but the one essential without which success is impossible is a thorough understanding of human reactions. Products are a dime a dozen. A person who has uncovered a hot market can always find a product to fill the needs of that market.

**How to Implement**: 
- Research your audience before studying your product
- Identify what they care about most
- Connect product features to audience needs
- Present product in terms of their desires, not your features
- Think of a typical individual, man or woman, who is likely to want what you sell
- Create a mental picture of one typical buyer
- Write your ad as if speaking to that one person

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Understanding that mothers think constantly about their babies, then connecting baby carriages to that concern
- **Good**: Knowing undertakers need black raincoats for funerals, then offering the solution
- **Good**: Writing to "a man on a Pullman using his favorite shaving soap" when selling shaving cream
- **Bad**: Writing to "men who shave" as an abstract group
- **Case Study**: "It was not raincoats we were selling. It was an idea... All we needed to understand was human reactions to certain ideas."
- **Case Study**: One ablest advertising man spent weeks selling from house to house before writing a single ad, learning reactions from different forms of argument and approach

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Starting with product features
- Assuming audience cares about what you care about
- Not researching audience psychology before writing
- Creating bland, generic appeals
- Using demographic data without understanding individual motivations
- Writing copy that sounds like a broadcast instead of a conversation

**Sources**: The Robert Collier Letter Book - Robert Collier, Scientific Advertising - Claude Hopkins

### Deep Product Research Before Writing

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
Study the product you are going to advertise extensively before writing a single word. The more you know about it, the more likely you are to come up with a big idea for selling it. An ad-writer must gain full information on his subject—often read for weeks on some problem. Perhaps in many volumes find few facts to use, but some one fact may be the keystone of success.

**Why It Matters**: 
Without deep product knowledge, you risk "skidding about on the slippery surface of irrelevant brilliance." Factual, informed copy sells better than creative but uninformed copy. Genius is the art of taking pains. The advertising man who spares the midnight oil will never get very far.

**How to Implement**: 
- Spend days or weeks reading about the product
- Interview engineers, developers, and product experts
- Use the product yourself
- Look for surprising or little-known facts that could become headlines
- Find the "inherent drama" in every product
- Read everything available about your product and category
- Interview hundreds of possible customers
- Study competitor claims and literature

**Examples**:
- **Good (Rolls-Royce)**: Ogilvy spent 3 weeks reading about Rolls-Royce and discovered "at sixty miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock"—which became the headline for one of the most famous car ads ever written
- **Good (Mercedes)**: A team spent 3 weeks in Stuttgart interviewing engineers. The resulting long-copy campaign increased US sales from 10,000 to 40,000 cars per year
- **Case Study (Coffee)**: Hopkins read enormous amounts on coffee—medical and otherwise—to advertise decaffeinated coffee. One scientific article out of a thousand gave the keynote: caffeine stimulation comes two hours after drinking, so immediate bracing effects don't come from caffeine. This reframed the product from "near-beer" to real coffee.
- **Bad**: Writing copy based on surface-level knowledge or assumptions about the product

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Being "too lazy to do this kind of homework"
- Relying on client briefings alone without independent research
- Assuming you know enough after a quick overview
- Writing without thorough research
- Stopping when you have "enough" information
- Not looking for the one unique insight that changes everything

**Sources**: Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy, Scientific Advertising - Claude Hopkins, Great Leads - Masterson & Forde

### The Counter-Argument: Sometimes Stay "Stupid"

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Mark Fenske advises: "Don't give into the temptation to take the factory tour. Resist. It makes you think like the client. You'll start to come up with the same answers the client does." Keeping your perspective fresh (tabula rasa) can lead to more original thinking.

**Why It Matters**: 
The customer only cares about "What's in it for me?"—not manufacturing details. There's a balance between product knowledge and customer perspective.

**How to Implement**: 
- Balance product knowledge with customer perspective
- Ask "What would make ME want this?" before "How is it made?"
- Stay connected to the customer's backyard reality
- Don't let technical knowledge overshadow customer benefits

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Writing from the customer's emotional perspective
- **Bad**: Getting lost in technical features that don't translate to benefits

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Becoming so product-focused you lose the customer view
- Writing like a product manual instead of a conversation
- Thinking like the client instead of the customer

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan (citing Mark Fenske)

### The BDF Formula: Beliefs, Desires, Feelings

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
To understand your prospect, ask three questions: What do they BELIEVE? What do they DESIRE? What do they FEEL? This simple framework replaces expensive market research and helps you get into the prospect's mind.

**Why It Matters**: 
Effective copy speaks to what's already in the prospect's mind. The BDF formula helps you get there without formal research.

**How to Implement**: 
- List your prospects' beliefs about your product category
- Identify their deepest desires related to your solution
- Understand their emotional state and frustrations
- Write copy that speaks directly to these beliefs, desires, and feelings

**Examples**:
- **IT Professionals**:
  - Believe: Technology is important; they're smarter than non-techies
  - Desire: Recognition, respect, job security, more money
  - Feel: Adversarial relationship with end users; resent explaining technology
- **Resulting headline**: "Important News for Any IT Professional Who Has Ever Felt Like Telling an End User, 'Go to Hell.'"
- **Case Study**: This BDF-based headline outperformed the original by 6 to 1

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Guessing instead of thinking deeply about your audience
- Focusing only on demographics instead of psychographics
- Ignoring the emotional state of your prospects

**Sources**: The Copywriter's Handbook - Robert Bly

### "Pathological Empathy" for Readers

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
Develop "pathological empathy" for your readers. Know them so well you can anticipate their questions, objections, and desires before they articulate them.

**Why It Matters**: 
Writing that resonates speaks directly to the reader's situation. Generic writing speaks to no one. The more deeply you understand your reader, the more persuasive your writing becomes.

**How to Implement**: 
- Create detailed reader personas based on real research, not assumptions
- Interview actual customers to understand their language and concerns
- Spend time where your readers spend time (forums, social media, reviews)
- Ask "What keeps them up at night?" and "What would make their life easier?"

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Using exact phrases from customer interviews in your headlines
- **Bad**: Writing what you think readers should want to hear
- **Case Study**: A B2B company interviews customers and discovers they don't care about "integration capabilities"—they care about "not having to re-enter data"

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Making assumptions about what readers want without research
- Writing for yourself or your boss instead of the actual reader
- Using company jargon instead of customer language

**Sources**: Everybody Writes - Ann Handley

### Research Consumer Language and Attitudes

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Find out how consumers think about your kind of product, what language they use when discussing the subject, what attributes are important to them, and what promise would be most likely to make them buy your brand.

**Why It Matters**: 
Using consumer language in your copy creates connection and credibility. Understanding what matters to them ensures your promise resonates.

**How to Implement**: 
- Conduct formal surveys or informal conversations
- Listen to how consumers naturally describe the product category
- Identify the attributes they value most
- Test different promises to find the most compelling one

**Examples**:
- **Case Study**: Informal conversations with half-a-dozen housewives can sometimes help a copywriter more than formal surveys in which he does not participate
- **Case Study (Shaving Cream)**: Before advertising shaving cream, one thousand men were asked what they most desired in shaving soap

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Using industry jargon instead of consumer language
- Assuming you know what matters to consumers without research
- Ignoring the "vocabulary consumers use"

**Sources**: Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy, Scientific Advertising - Claude Hopkins

### Survey Consumers to Find True Demand

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Canvas homes and dealers. Survey consumers. Sometimes you discover that what you planned to advertise isn't what the market actually needs.

**Why It Matters**: 
Advertising based on wrong assumptions about the market must fail. Research reveals the true opportunity.

**How to Implement**: 
- Survey homes before launching campaigns
- Survey dealers to understand their perspective
- Measure actual behavior, not just stated preferences
- Let research reshape your advertising approach

**Examples**:
- **Case Study (Pork and Beans)**: Called to advertise pork and beans, a canvass of thousands of homes showed that only 4% used any canned pork and beans—96% baked their own. The problem wasn't selling a brand (appealing to 4%), but winning people away from home-baked beans. The advertising succeeded because of this insight.
- **Case Study (Food Product)**: Before advertising a food product, 130 men were employed for weeks interviewing all classes of consumers.

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Assuming you know the market
- Advertising to the wrong problem
- Not validating assumptions before spending

**Sources**: Scientific Advertising - Claude Hopkins

### The Desire Framework: What People Want

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Schwab provides comprehensive lists of what people want:

**People Want to GAIN**: Health, Time, Money, Popularity, Improved appearance, Security in old age, Praise from others, Comfort, Leisure, Pride of accomplishment, Advancement (business, social), Increased enjoyment, Self-confidence, Personal prestige

**People Want to BE**: Good parents, Sociable/hospitable, Up-to-date, Creative, Proud of their possessions, Influential over others, Gregarious, Efficient, "First" in things, Recognized as authorities

**People Want to DO**: Express their personalities, Resist domination by others, Satisfy their curiosity, Emulate the admirable, Appreciate beauty, Acquire or collect things, Win others' affection, Improve themselves generally

**People Want to SAVE**: Time, Money, Work, Discomfort, Worry, Doubts, Risks, Personal embarrassment

**Why It Matters**: 
If you tie up the advantages of your product with what people want to gain, be, do, and save, you will make them want to buy.

**How to Implement**: 
- Review this list for each product
- Identify which desires your product satisfies
- Feature the most powerful desires prominently
- Use both positive (gain) and negative (avoid) appeals

**Examples**:
- **Better Health**: "Greater strength, vigor, endurance. The possibility of longer life."
- **More Money**: "For spending, saving, or giving to others"
- **Social Advancement**: "Moving in better circles. Social acceptance."

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Focusing on minor desires when major ones apply
- Missing the emotional desires behind practical purchases

**Sources**: How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab

### Modern Psychological Trends to Consider

**Principle**: 
Modern trends show preference for:
- Success over Integrity
- Spending over Saving
- Restlessness over Rest
- Self-Indulgence over Self-Discipline
- Desire for the New over Affection for the Old
- Show over Solidity
- Dependence over Self-Reliance
- Gregariousness over Solitude
- Luxury over Simplicity

**Why It Matters**: 
Like it or not, these trends must be considered when writing copy based on mass appeal.

**How to Implement**: 
- Don't fight against these trends

- Acknowledge these realities while crafting appeals

**Examples**:
- **Aligned with Trends**: Emphasize instant gratification, social proof, luxury benefits
- **Against the Grain**: Positioning for "careful thought" may reach smaller audience

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Writing copy for how you think people should be
- Ignoring the actual psychology of the mass market

### Use Exact Figures from Testing

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Impressive claims are made far more impressive by making them exact. Conduct experiments to get actual figures rather than general statements. Specific numbers from testing have more credibility and impact than general assertions.

**Why It Matters**: 
Specifics work better than generalities. Use percentages, time elapsed, dollars saved. You are talking to people who appreciate precision.

**How to Implement**: 
- Test products to get specific numbers
- Verify manufacturer claims with independent testing
- Convert qualitative descriptions to quantitative facts
- Use laboratory results when available

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "Food value is 425 calories per pint. One pint equals six eggs in calories."
- **Bad**: "High food value"
- **Good**: "Sears makes a profit of 5 per cent"—specific and persuasive
- **Bad**: "Sears' profit is less than you might suppose"—vague and unconvincing
- **Case Study**: Chemists are often employed to prove or disprove doubtful claims. It is remarkable how often makers prove wrong on assertions they had made for years.

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Accepting manufacturer claims without verification
- Using qualitative language when quantitative is available
- Not testing to find specific, impressive numbers

**Sources**: Scientific Advertising - Claude Hopkins, Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy

### Avoid "Country Club Thinking"

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
The more successful an advertising person becomes, the more they risk "country-club thinking"—losing the down-to-earth understanding of ordinary people whose mass response made them successful.

**Why It Matters**: 
"It is very nice to be an executive, but to the extent you climb up, you tend to leave behind the people on whom all power depends... If you are not careful you will forget those plain people from whom all your strength must come."

**How to Implement**: 
Five ways to stay connected:
1. **Read**: Comics, newspaper features, stories with large popular following
2. **See**: Smash-hit movies, TV shows, plays attracting millions
3. **Listen**: Radio programs holding interest of countless people
4. **Talk**: With taxi drivers, laborers, newsboys, clerks outside rarefied heights
5. **Study**: Mail-order ads that repeat because they pay in direct results

**Examples**:
- **Danger**: "My wife says..." is one of advertising's most expensive phrases—unrepresentative opinions

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Testing copy on sophisticated friends
- "Over-Ritzing" advertising for mass-market products
- Assuming your taste matches your audience's

**Sources**: How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab

### Universal Human Appeals Transcend Nationality

**Type**: Tip

**Principle**: 
Basic human appeals transcend nationality. "The Johns, Jacques, Giovannis, Juans, Jonathans are all simply Joneses underneath." Human nature hasn't changed for a billion years and won't vary in the next billion years. Only the superficial things have changed.

**Why It Matters**: 
A communicator must be concerned with unchanging man—what compulsions drive him, what instincts dominate his every action. The creative man with an insight into human nature, with the artistry to touch and move people, will succeed.

**How to Implement**: 
- Focus on universal human desires
- Adapt language but keep appeals
- Test before assuming "different market"

**Examples**:
- **Case Study (Schwab)**: When American copy that worked spectacularly in the US was adapted for England and France (simply removing Americanisms), it produced $498,000 in receipts on $60,000 in advertising spend. The British agency said American approach wouldn't work—they were wrong.

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Assuming basic appeals differ by nationality
- Rewriting everything for new markets instead of adapting
- Dismissing proven copy without testing

**Sources**: How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab, Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy (citing Bill Bernbach)

### The List Quality Hierarchy

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Mailing list quality follows a specific hierarchy. The best list is always your own satisfied customer list. Beyond that, prioritize lists based on recency, frequency, unit of sale, proven mail order buying behavior, and similarity to your offer.

**Why It Matters**: 
The difference between mailing to a mediocre list versus a hot list can be the difference between losing money and making millions. List selection is often more important than the copy itself.

**How to Implement**: 
From worst to best, rank your list options:
1. Phone book names (terrible—no common traits except having a phone)
2. High-income area residents (slightly better but too much waste)
3. Proven affluent professionals (doctors, lawyers, etc.)
4. Proven mail order buyers of anything
5. Wealthy mail order buyers
6. People who bought similar products by mail
7. Repeat buyers of similar products by mail
8. Repeat buyers who paid high prices for similar products
9. Recent buyers matching all above criteria
10. Lists your broker says are working for similar offers
11. YOUR OWN SATISFIED CUSTOMER LIST (best of all)

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Mailing an investment book to wealthy people who have purchased (by mail) similar investment books multiple times, recently, for high prices
- **Bad**: Mailing an investment book to names from a telephone directory
- **Case Study**: The Coat-of-Arms promotion was mailed to names from telephone white pages and still succeeded because of the massive scale and universal appeal—but this is the exception, not the rule

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Treating all mailing lists as equal
- Prioritizing list size over list quality
- Ignoring recency as the most important factor
- Not asking your list broker what's working for similar offers

**Sources**: The Boron Letters - Gary Halbert

---

### Recency, Frequency, and Unit of Sale (RFU)

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
When evaluating mailing lists (or any audience), use three main guidelines: Recency (how recently they bought), Frequency (how often they buy), and Unit of Sale (how much they spend). Of these three, recency is by far the most important.

**Why It Matters**: 
A recent buyer is actively in buying mode. Their attention is focused, their wallet is open, and their interest is high. This psychological state fades with time.

**How to Implement**: 
- **Recency**: Always ask for "hotline buyers"—90-day or 30-day recent purchasers. Get them while they're hot!
- **Frequency**: Multiple purchases indicate genuine interest, not impulse buying
- **Unit of Sale**: Someone who paid $100 for diet pills is a hotter prospect than someone who paid $10

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Renting a list of 30-day hotline buyers who have purchased multiple skin diving books
- **Bad**: Renting a general list of people who once bought something vaguely related years ago
- **Case Study**: Gary emphasizes that if you're selling a book on skin diving and find people who have purchased several other skin diving books, you know they're genuinely interested

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Ignoring recency in favor of lower list rental costs
- Assuming all buyers are equally valuable regardless of when they bought
- Not asking about hotline segments of lists

**Sources**: The Boron Letters - Gary Halbert

### Your Own Customer List Is Your Best Asset

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
All other things being equal, your own satisfied customer list will respond far better than any other list you can rent. This is your most valuable marketing asset. The easiest person to sell is one who has previously bought similar products by mail.

**Why It Matters**: 
Your customers already know you, trust you, and have proven they will spend money with you. The relationship is established, making future sales significantly easier.

**How to Implement**: 
- Build and maintain a customer list from day one
- Segment customers by what they bought, when, and how much
- Create backend offers specifically for existing customers
- Keep customers satisfied—the caveat is they must be satisfied customers

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Creating new products and offers specifically for people who already bought from you
- **Bad**: Constantly chasing new customers while ignoring existing ones
- **Case Study (Coat-of-Arms)**: The letter generated 7.156 million cash-with-order customers. The real goldmine was selling additional products (wall plaques, etc.) to those existing customers—generating $40 million more
- **Case Study (Collier)**: Sold 50,000 traveling bags primarily by mailing to book buyer lists—people who had proven they would buy by mail

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Focusing all energy on new customer acquisition
- Not collecting and maintaining customer data
- Treating past customers as one-time transactions
- Neglecting customer satisfaction

**Sources**: The Boron Letters - Gary Halbert, The Robert Collier Letter Book - Robert Collier

### Heavy Users Drive the Market

**Type**: Metric

**Principle**: 
Heavy users account for the vast majority of consumption. In everything you do, keep your eye glued to the heavy users. They are unlike occasional users in their motivations.

**Statistics**:
- 32% of beer drinkers drink 80% of all beer
- 23% of laxative users consume 80% of all laxatives
- 14% of gin drinkers consume 80% of all gin

**Why It Matters**: 
Heavy users drive revenue. Understanding them is disproportionately important.

**How to Implement**: 
- Identify your heavy users
- Study their motivations (different from light users)
- Target advertising to them
- Don't treat all customers equally

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Averaging all users together
- Targeting light users to "grow the market"
- Ignoring heavy user insights

**Sources**: Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy

### Personalize by Profession, Industry, or Interest

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
People respond best when they feel you understand their specific situation. Personalizing letters by occupation, profession, or interest creates the feeling that you specialize in THEIR needs.

**Why It Matters**: 
When John Blair of the New Process Company started by selling only to undertakers, they felt he understood their unique problem (needing black raincoats for funerals). This personalized approach built his business from $100 to $2,000,000.

**How to Implement**: 
- Write different letters for different professions/interests
- Quote testimonials from people in the same field
- Reference specific problems unique to that group
- Use local names and references when possible

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "The best value in a Raincoat I have ever had." The very words of John J. Smith, of 2601 Racine Avenue of your city. Perhaps you know Mr. Smith.
- **Good**: Listing local customers by name and address
- **Case Study**: John Blair built a $2,000,000 business by personalizing to each profession

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Sending generic letters to everyone
- Using testimonials from unrelated industries
- Missing local personalization opportunities

**Sources**: The Robert Collier Letter Book - Robert Collier

### Know Your Target's Gender and Demographics

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Know your target audience's gender and avoid language or examples that might alienate them. Understanding your actual buyer demographics is essential for effective targeting.

**Why It Matters**: 
Misunderstanding your audience's gender—or using sexist language—can destroy response rates and damage your brand.

**How to Implement**: 
- Research your actual buyer demographics
- Avoid gender stereotypes unless intentionally targeting
- Be aware of language that might offend
- Understand what matters to different audiences

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Understanding that women typically focus on color, fashion, relationships while men focus on specs, achievement, supporting family—then writing appropriately
- **Bad**: Using sexist humor in copy that reaches a female audience
- **Case Study**: Sugarman received angry letters from women about an ad they perceived as sexist

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Not knowing your actual audience gender
- Using gender stereotypes inappropriately
- Not considering how different genders respond to different appeals

**Sources**: The Adweek Copywriting Handbook - Joseph Sugarman

### Understand the Emotional Nature of Your Prospects

**Type**: Trigger

**Principle**: 
Understand the emotional nature and motivations of your prospects. Match your presentation to their deep psychological needs. Go beyond demographics. Feel the customer's situation. Find the emotion, and you'll be miles ahead of someone who's just thinking about the brand.

**Why It Matters**: 
Knowing why prospects buy is more important than knowing what you're selling. Emotional understanding drives effective copy. People buy emotionally and justify rationally.

**How to Implement**: 
- Research prospect motivations
- Understand emotional needs being fulfilled
- Match presentation to psychological drivers
- Connect product benefits to emotional desires
- Imagine a day in the life of your customer
- Consider their fears, hopes, and pain points
- Find the emotional core of the buying decision

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Understanding fraternity prospects wanted girls and brotherhood—then creating illusion of both
- **Bad**: Selling based on features without understanding emotional motivations
- **Case Study**: Sugarman transformed worst fraternity on campus by understanding why people join (girls + camaraderie) and creating that illusion

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Focusing only on product features
- Not researching prospect psychology
- Assuming logical appeals drive purchase

**Sources**: The Adweek Copywriting Handbook - Joseph Sugarman, Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

### Don't Under-Sell to Quality Audiences

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
"The colonel's lady and Judy O'Grady are sisters under their skins." (Kipling) Don't pull punches when selling to quality audiences—affluent people are usually MORE careful buyers, need even more facts and persuasion than those below them socially.

**Why It Matters**: 
Surveys show low-income families produce the LOWEST percentage of coupon redemption for 5¢, 10¢, 15¢ savings. Richer people respond more to bargains.

**How to Implement**: 
- Don't assume quality audiences need less selling
- Include strong selling points regardless of audience sophistication
- Advertising that doesn't get under the epidermis of position misses sales

**Examples**:
- **Wrong assumption**: Delicate, reticent copy for "quality" products
- **Reality**: No reader can be influenced by good sales angles not in the ad at all

**Common Mistakes**: 
- "Quality" advertising that's too genteel to sell
- Assuming rich people don't need persuasion
- Leaving out selling points to seem sophisticated

**Sources**: How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab

### The Two Products in Every Product

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Every product you sell is actually two products:
1. **Physical Product** - The steel, glass, paper, or material the manufacturer has shaped
2. **Functional Product** - The product in action, the series of benefits it performs for the consumer

People don't buy the physical product. They buy what it DOES.

**Why It Matters**: 
The physical product only justifies your price; the functional product creates the sale.

**How to Implement**: 
- List all performances/functions your product contains
- Group these performances against mass desires each satisfies
- Feature the ONE performance that harnesses greatest sales power
- Use physical product facts only to document and reinforce the functional promise

**Examples**:
- **Physical**: Weight of steel in car door
- **Functional**: Protection of your life in an accident
- **Application**: "The weight of steel in your car's door" documents "This car will protect your life"

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Leading with physical product attributes
- Featuring plant size, construction quality, or materials without connecting to benefits
- Confusing what the product IS with what it DOES

**Sources**: Breakthrough Advertising - Eugene Schwartz

### Understand the Nature of Your Product

**Type**: Trigger

**Principle**: 
Every product has a unique personality and nature. Understanding it determines how to present it most effectively. "There is an inherent drama in every product. Our No. 1 job is to dig for it and capitalize on it."

**Why It Matters**: 
A toy has different nature than a medical device. Presenting products against their nature fails; presenting with their nature succeeds.

**How to Implement**: 
- Study the product's inherent personality
- Present products consistent with their nature
- Match tone, approach, and environment to product nature
- Use common sense about what the product represents
- Dig for the inherent drama
- Don't settle for the obvious
- Reach for excellence

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Toy presented with fun and enjoyment; medical device presented seriously and credibly
- **Bad**: Trying to make a serious product silly or a fun product serious
- **Case Study**: Burglar alarm understood as serious product needing reliability emphasis, not scare tactics

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Not identifying product nature
- Presenting products against their nature
- Using inappropriate tone or approach

**Sources**: The Adweek Copywriting Handbook - Joseph Sugarman, Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy (citing Leo Burnett)

## 4. Competitive Analysis

### Study Competitor Advertising

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
Find out what kind of advertising your competitors have been doing for similar products, and with what success. This will give you your bearings.

**Why It Matters**: 
Understanding the competitive landscape helps you position your product distinctly and avoid repeating others' mistakes.

**How to Implement**: 
- Collect competitor advertisements
- Analyze their strategies, promises, and executions
- Note what appears to be working (repeated campaigns) vs. abandoned approaches
- Look for opportunities to differentiate
- Study repeat mail order ads—if they keep running, they're working

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Identifying that competitors use short-term pricing while you could build long-term brand value

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Copying competitor approaches without understanding why they work
- Ignoring the competitive landscape entirely

**Sources**: Ogilvy on Advertising - David Ogilvy

---

### Attack the Weakness in the Leader's Strength

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
"Find a weakness in the leader's strength and attack at that point." Turn the competitor's biggest advantage into their vulnerability. Direct competition on the leader's terms rarely works—flanking moves and strategic reframing create opportunities.

**Why It Matters**: 
Direct frontal assault on the market leader rarely works. Finding an angle that turns their strength into a weakness allows smaller competitors to gain market share.

**How to Implement**: 
- Identify the leader's main strength
- Find the inherent weakness in that strength
- Position your brand as the solution to that weakness
- Make the comparison implicit, not explicit

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Avis: "We're #2, so the line at our counter is shorter" (turns Hertz's dominance into a disadvantage)
- **Bad**: Directly claiming "We're better than [Leader]" without proof
- **Case Study (Heinz)**: Heinz ketchup claimed "slowest" when competitors fought over "tomato-iest"—sales went up and stayed up

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Attacking the leader head-on without strategic advantage
- Ignoring the competitive landscape entirely
- Making claims without substantiation

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

---

### Your Brand Must Own One Adjective

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
Make your brand stand for one thing. Pair it with one adjective. Brand = adjective.

**Why It Matters**: 
With 85,000+ brands competing for customer attention, customers mentally file brands using a single word. Volvo owns "safe" even though it's no longer in the top 10 safest cars—such is the power of simplicity.

**How to Implement**: 
- Strip your brand's value proposition down to the bone
- Find the one adjective that defines your brand
- Make everything you do adhere to draconian standards of simplicity
- If good adjectives are taken, try a polar opposite or flanking move

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Jeep = "tough," Porsche = "fast," BMW = "performance," Volvo = "safe"
- **Bad**: Trying to claim multiple adjectives or settling for second-best ("Quenching" when "Refreshing" is taken)
- **Case Study**: Heinz ketchup claimed "slowest" when competitors fought over "tomato-iest"—sales went up and stayed up

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Trying to communicate multiple brand benefits simultaneously
- Settling for a weak adjective because the strong ones are taken
- Abandoning your adjective when secondary benefits seem important

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

---

### The Importance of a Tight Strategy

**Type**: Rule

**Principle**: 
Tight strategies seem to be the exception, not the rule, but you must push for one. A vague strategy produces vague work. A tight strategy focuses creative energy.

**Why It Matters**: 
Without a clear target, even talented creatives waste effort. Strategy provides the constraints that enable creativity.

**How to Implement**: 
- Push back on vague briefs
- Ask clarifying questions until strategy is sharp
- Look for the single most important thing to communicate
- Don't start creating until strategy is tight

**Examples**:
- **Good**: A brief with one clear, focused message
- **Bad**: A brief with multiple objectives and audiences

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Accepting weak briefs without pushback
- Starting creative with fuzzy strategy
- Trying to solve multiple problems at once

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

### Instead of Advertising Ideas, Create Ideas Worth Advertising

**Type**: Guideline

**Principle**: 
"Instead of coming up with advertising ideas, come up with ideas worth advertising." Sometimes the best creative contribution is making the product or experience better, not just communicating about it.

**Why It Matters**: 
No amount of brilliant advertising can save a bad product. Sometimes the creative opportunity is in the product itself. A gifted product is mightier than a gifted pen.

**How to Implement**: 
- Look for ways to improve the product/service
- Think beyond communication to experience
- Consider: What would make this worth talking about?
- Be willing to make suggestions beyond advertising

**Examples**:
- **Good**: Suggesting product improvements that make advertising easier
- **Bad**: Trying to advertise your way out of a product problem

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Limiting creative thinking to communication
- Accepting the product as unchangeable
- Not considering the full customer experience

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

---

### Problem Framing: Start with "How Might We...?"

**Type**: Framework

**Principle**: 
Start with more interesting questions: Try "How might we...?" This reframes problems as opportunities and opens up creative possibilities.

**Why It Matters**: 
The question you ask shapes the answers you get. Better questions lead to better solutions.

**How to Implement**: 
- Reframe briefs as "How might we...?" questions
- Make questions open-ended and ambitious
- Use questions to expand possibilities
- Let questions guide exploration

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "How might we make waiting in line enjoyable?" vs. "Write a queue management ad"
- **Bad**: Starting with "We need a commercial about..."

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Accepting the brief's framing without question
- Asking narrow, closed questions
- Not exploring multiple ways to frame the problem

**Sources**: Hey, Whipple, Squeeze This - Luke Sullivan

---

### The Obvious Can Be Powerful

**Type**: Tip

**Principle**: 
"An ability to see the obvious and do it." Never discard an idea because it's "too obvious" unless there's another good reason. The more obvious things are, the more we lose sight of them—simply because we know them so well.

**Why It Matters**: 
Sometimes the most powerful selling ideas are so obvious the experts overlook them.

**How to Implement**: 
- Don't dismiss simple, obvious approaches
- Ask: "Is this obvious because it's wrong, or obvious because it's right?"
- Pierce through miasma of irrelevancies to the most motivational idea

**Examples**:
- **Obvious Adams**: His thinking was never circuitous—he focused on what would later prove to be the most motivational selling idea. "It was so darned obvious! Why didn't I ever think of it?"

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Rejecting obvious solutions as too simple
- Overcomplicating when straightforward would work

**Sources**: How to Write a Good Advertisement - Victor O. Schwab

### The 10 Smart Market Diagnosis and Profiling Questions

**Type**: Framework
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
Before writing a single word, answer these 10 questions about your customer to truly understand who you're writing to.

**Why It Matters**: 
You can't persuade someone you don't understand. These questions reveal emotional triggers, decision-making patterns, and the language of your market.

**How to Implement**: 
Answer each question thoroughly before writing:
1. What keeps them awake at night, indigestion boiling up their esophagus, eyes open, staring at the ceiling?
2. What are they afraid of?
3. What are they angry about? Who are they angry at?
4. What are their top three daily frustrations?
5. What trends are occurring and will occur in their businesses or lives?
6. What do they secretly, ardently desire most?
7. Is there a built-in bias to the way they make decisions? (Example: engineers = exceptionally analytical)
8. Do they have their own language?
9. Who else is selling something similar to them, and how?
10. Who else has tried selling them something similar, and how has that effort failed?

**Common Mistakes**: 
- Skipping this step because you "already know" your customers
- Using demographic data alone without understanding emotional drivers
- Failing to learn your customer's specialized language

### Walk a Mile in Their Shoes

**Type**: Guideline
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
You cannot persuade someone until you truly understand them. Get out of your office and into your customer's world.

**Why It Matters**: 
Writers who stay in ivory towers create tone-deaf copy. Those who immerse themselves in their customer's reality write copy that resonates.

**How to Implement**: 
- Drive through neighborhoods of your target Zip Codes
- Attend their trade meetings and conventions
- Read their trade journals and magazines
- Eavesdrop on their conversations
- Take customers to lunch and interview them
- Visit discussion forums where they communicate
- "Play prospect" with competitors

**Case Study**: Jerry Jones (non-dentist) became a successful marketing guru to dentists by: reading EVERY industry publication monthly, visiting web forums for dentists, subscribing to dentist email groups, attending conventions, "playing prospect" with other vendors, and even investing in dental practices.

### Address Customer Priorities, Not Your Own

**Type**: Rule
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
Write about what matters to the customer, not what matters to you. The customer's priorities determine what gets read and what gets results.

**Why It Matters**: 
The classic sales failure: telling customers everything about what's important to YOU without addressing what's important to THEM.

**How to Implement**: 
- List your customer's priorities in order of importance
- Lead with their #1 concern
- Answer "Will this thing keep a little old lady warm?" before discussing BTUs

**Case Study**: A charity fundraising letter that addressed donor priorities: "What benefit to me justifies the cost? Who else has given? How would I get the money?" Result: One new donor contributed $13,000—half the telethon's costs.

### The "Theater In Your Mind" Visualization Technique

### Build a List of Features AND Benefits

**Type**: Guideline
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
Before writing, list every feature of your product/service—then translate each into a benefit. People don't buy things for what they ARE; they buy things for what they DO.

**Why It Matters**: 
Features describe the product. Benefits describe what it does for the buyer. Only benefits motivate purchase.

**How to Implement**: 
- Use 3x5 cards, one item per card
- Write all features and benefits
- Sort by importance
- Pin them up where you can see them while writing

**Examples**:
- **Feature**: "Has 12 attachments" → **Benefit**: "Cleans every surface in your home without switching equipment"
- **Feature**: "72% pure silver content" → **Benefit**: "As silver prices climb, value should skyrocket—risk insurance on your investment"

### The Hidden Benefit Technique

**Type**: Tip
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
Look for the "hidden benefit"—not the obvious first benefit, but one of profound importance that isn't immediately apparent.

**Why It Matters**: 
Hidden benefits often resonate more deeply because they address what customers truly want but may not openly admit.

**Case Study**: Insurance executives at a recruiting seminar spent breaks talking about golf. The hidden benefit of the recruiting system became: "Puts Recruiting On Auto-Pilot So You Can Go Play Golf!" This became one of the most profitable ads ever run.

### Create a Damaging Admission

**Type**: Technique
**Source**: The Ultimate Sales Letter - Dan Kennedy

**Principle**: 
Identify and openly discuss the flaws in your product, service, or offer. By acknowledging drawbacks, you force yourself to address objections and enhance credibility.

**Why It Matters**: 
Nothing's perfect and everybody knows that. Admitting weaknesses builds trust and prevents skepticism from derailing sales.

**How to Implement**: 
- List every reason someone might NOT respond
- Address each concern directly in your copy
- Turn disadvantages into advantages where possible

**Example**: Italian restaurant letter: "If you want waiters in tuxedos with white linen cloths... our little restaurant is not the place to come. But if you mostly want good, solid, home-cooked pasta with tasty sauces made by a real Italian Mama, and will trade white linen for red and white checked plastic tablecloths, you'll like our place just fine."

### The Smallest Viable Audience

**Type**: Framework
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
Write for the smallest group of people you need to serve, not the largest audience you could reach. The more specific your audience, the more powerful your copy becomes.

**Why It Matters**: 
Mass marketing creates bland copy that resonates with no one. By focusing on a specific group, you can speak their language, address their exact problems, and create tension that actually moves them to act. The relentless pursuit of mass makes you boring.

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- Begin every piece by answering: "Who is this for specifically?"
- Use language, references, and examples that only resonate with your specific audience
- Don't dilute your message trying to appeal to everyone
- If your copy could work for anyone, it works for no one

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "For freelance designers billing $5K+ per project who are tired of scope creep destroying their margins"
- **Bad**: "For anyone who wants to make more money" (too broad, no specificity)

### The "People Like Us" Identity Frame

**Type**: Framework
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
Frame your copy around tribal identity: "People like us do things like this." Readers make decisions based on whether an action aligns with their self-image and peer group behavior.

**Why It Matters**: 
We don't make decisions in isolation—we base them on our perception of our cohort. Copy that aligns with the reader's identity gets adopted; copy that conflicts with it gets rejected. This is the most fundamental driver of human behavior.

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- Open with identity-affirming statements: "For entrepreneurs who refuse to settle..."
- Use inclusive language that creates an "us": "We both know that..."
- Reference behaviors of the aspirational peer group
- Make the reader feel seen and understood before asking for action
- Show that others like them have already made this choice

**Example**: "Smart marketers like you know that the old playbook doesn't work anymore. That's why thousands of growth-focused teams have switched to [Product]."

### The Internal Narrative

**Type**: Guideline
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
Everyone operates from an internal narrative—a story they tell themselves about who they are and how the world works. Your copy must align with (not contradict) this narrative to succeed.

**Why It Matters**: 
You can't persuade someone their internal narrative is wrong. Their story is completely true to them. Instead of fighting it, speak to it. Show how your offer fits within the story they already believe.

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- Research what your audience tells themselves about their situation
- Validate their current beliefs before introducing new information
- Frame your solution as consistent with their existing worldview
- Never make them feel stupid for their current choices
- Position your offer as the obvious next step in their journey

**Examples**:
- **Good**: "You've always known that hustle alone isn't the answer. Smart work beats hard work every time. Here's the system that lets your best ideas actually get implemented."
- **Bad**: "Everything you've been doing is wrong. Here's the truth." (attacks their narrative, creates resistance)

### "It's Not for You" — The Power of Exclusion

**Type**: Rule
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
Having the courage to say "It's not for you" makes your copy stronger. Exclusion signals respect—you're not wasting anyone's time, and you're serious about serving those you've chosen.

**Why It Matters**: 
When you try to please everyone, you please no one. Exclusion creates belonging for those who are included. It signals confidence and builds trust.

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- State clearly who this is for AND who it's not for
- Use qualifying language: "If you're serious about...", "For those ready to..."
- Don't apologize for exclusion—state it confidently
- The more specifically you exclude, the more included your real audience feels

**Example**: "This isn't for hobbyists or tire-kickers. This is for professional photographers who are ready to triple their booking rate in the next 90 days."

### Empathy Is Seeing Their Fear

**Type**: Guideline
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
When someone doesn't act as expected, look for their fear. People don't buy what you're selling because they're afraid—of wasting money, looking stupid, being scammed, or making a mistake. Address the fear.

**Why It Matters**: 
Most objections are fear in disguise. "It's too expensive" often means "I'm afraid it won't work." "I need to think about it" often means "I'm afraid of making a mistake."

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- List the fears your prospect has before writing
- Address fears directly, not just benefits
- Use social proof to reduce "looking stupid" fear
- Use guarantees to reduce "wasting money" fear
- Use specificity to reduce "being scammed" fear

**Example**: "You might be thinking: 'What if this doesn't work for my industry?' Fair question. Here are 47 case studies from businesses exactly like yours."

### Sonder and the Noise in Their Heads

**Type**: Guideline
**Source**: This Is Marketing - Seth Godin

**Principle**: 
Sonder is the realization that everyone around you has an internal life as rich and conflicted as yours. Your reader has their own fears, hopes, and noise in their head that has nothing to do with you.

**Why It Matters**: 
We assume people will give our message full attention. They won't. They're dealing with a thousand other things. Your copy competes with their internal narrative, not just other ads.

**How to Implement in Copy**: 
- Keep copy scannable—they won't read every word
- Assume they're distracted and skeptical
- Meet them where they are emotionally, not where you want them to be
- Acknowledge their reality before introducing yours

**Example**: "You're busy. You've got a million things on your plate. So let me get straight to the point: [value proposition]."

### Godin's Marketing Worksheet (Copywriting Adaptation)

Use these questions before writing any major copy piece:

1. **Who's it for?** (Be specific—smallest viable audience)
2. **What's it for?** (What change are you making?)
3. **What is their worldview?** (What do they already believe?)
4. **What are they afraid of?** (Address the fear directly)
5. **What story will you tell?** (Is it true? Is it consistent?)
6. **How will it change their status?** (Affiliation or dominance?)
7. **How will you reach the early adopters?** (The neophiliacs who go first)
8. **Why will they tell their friends?** (What's in it for them to share?)
9. **What will they tell their friends?** (Give them the words)
10. **Where's the network effect?** (Does it get better with more users?)
11. **Are you proud of it?** (If not, don't ship it)

## Additions from Ray Edwards (How to Write Copy That Sells)

### Copywriting Is "Salesmanship in Print"

**Type**: Principle
**Source**: How to Write Copy That Sells - Ray Edwards

**Principle**:
Copywriting is the writing of ads, and an ad is "salesmanship in print" (John E. Kennedy's three-word definition given to Albert Lasker in 1904). "Print" now extends to web pages, videos, podcasts, social media, and email, but the principle is unchanged: copy is a salesperson working at scale.

**Why It Matters**:
Edwards calls copywriting "the most pivotal and essential business skill you can learn" — the lever behind nearly all internet-based businesses. More than product, traffic, list size, or JV partners, it is copy that drives revenue. Done well, it can increase top-line sales while decreasing advertising cost, raising bottom-line profit.

**How to Implement**:
- Treat every piece of communication (sales letter, email, Facebook ad, product listing) as "an ad"
- Apply face-to-face salesmanship principles to the written word
- Invest in the skill as a durable business asset, not a "shiny object" tactic

---

### Persuasion vs. Manipulation (The Copywriter's Ethic)

**Type**: Principle
**Source**: How to Write Copy That Sells - Ray Edwards

**Principle**:
Manipulation is using tactics to move people toward something that is NOT in their best interest and that they did not want. Persuasion is using tactics to move people toward something that IS in their best interest and that they wanted to begin with. The same psychological tools can do either; the copywriter must choose persuasion. The word "pastor" originally meant "to shepherd" — to care for, feed, and protect the flock — and Edwards frames the copywriter's role the same way.

**Why It Matters**:
These tools are genuinely powerful enough to "manipulate people's thinking and actions." Using them only for the customer's good is both an ethical commitment and a long-term business strategy — manipulation eventually destroys reputation and lifetime value.

**How to Implement**:
- Only sell things you believe genuinely help the buyer
- Make a personal commitment to never use these tactics against a customer's interest
- Treat customers as a flock to be served, not a herd to be exploited

**Common Mistakes**:
- Rationalizing manipulative tactics because "they work"

### The "Copy Thesis" / Big Idea One-Sentence Template

**Type**: Framework
**Source**: How to Write Copy That Sells - Ray Edwards

**Principle**:
Before writing, distill what you're selling and how it benefits the customer into a single, crystal-clear sentence — the "Copy Thesis" or big idea — using this template:

> Any **[YOUR AUDIENCE]** can **[SOLVE THEIR PROBLEM]** by using **[YOUR PRODUCT]**, because **[HOW IT SOLVES THE PROBLEM]**.

**Why It Matters**:
A thesis is "a proposition to be discussed and proved." Edwards cites preacher J.H. Jowett: no sermon is ready "until we can express its theme in a short, pregnant sentence as clear as a crystal." Clarifying the big idea is the crucial first step of selling — everything else flows from it. (Complements the "Big Idea" and "Rule of One" material already in this knowledge base by giving an explicit fill-in-the-blank format.)

**Examples**:
- "Any PARENT can IMPROVE THEIR CHILD'S BEHAVIOR by using YOUR BEST KID NOW, because IT MAKES GOOD BEHAVIOR AUTOMATIC."
- "Any OBESE PERSON can BECOME LEAN & FIT by following THE FREE FROM FAT FOREVER DIET, because it USES YOUR BODY'S BUILT-IN FAT-BURNING FURNACE."

**Common Mistakes**:
- Starting to write before the big idea is rock-solid
- A vague thesis that can't be stated in one clear sentence

### The O.P.E.N. Scale of Buying Awareness

**Type**: Framework
**Source**: How to Write Copy That Sells - Ray Edwards

**Principle**:
Choose your offer and approach based on the prospect's stage of buying awareness. O.P.E.N.:

- **O — Oblivious**: doesn't know they have a problem or that you exist; hardest to sell to
- **P — Pondering**: starting to feel the problem; beginning to think about a solution
- **E — Engaged**: actively has the problem and is looking for a solution before it worsens
- **N — Need**: in agony (the migraine sufferer); will take anything that solves the problem; easiest to sell to

**Why It Matters**:
A complement to Schwartz's Five Stages of Market Awareness (see Part 1). O.P.E.N. maps awareness onto offer choice: the Oblivious prospect needs a low-commitment Soft Offer, while the Need prospect converts on a simple Hard Offer.

**Example**:
A headache remedy: the Oblivious person has never had a headache; the Pondering person feels the first twinges; the Engaged person has a mild headache and is searching; the Need person has a migraine and will buy anything promising relief.
